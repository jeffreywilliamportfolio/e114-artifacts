Photosynthesis is the biological process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy. This process occurs primarily in the **chloroplasts** of plant cells and can be divided into two main stages: the **Light-Dependent Reactions** and the **Calvin Cycle** (Light-Independent Reactions).

Here is a step-by-step breakdown of how it works:

### 1. The Light-Dependent Reactions
These reactions occur in the **thylakoid membranes** of the chloroplasts. Their primary goal is to capture light energy and convert it into chemical energy carriers (ATP and NADPH).

*   **Absorption of Light:** Chlorophyll and other pigments in the photosystems (PSII and PSI) absorb photons from sunlight.
*   **Water Splitting (Photolysis):** In Photosystem II (PSII), the absorbed energy excites electrons. To replace these lost electrons, water molecules ($H_2O$) are split. This releases:
    *   **Oxygen ($O_2$):** Released as a byproduct into the atmosphere.
    *   **Protons ($H^+$):** Pumped into the thylakoid lumen.
    *   **Electrons:** Passed through an electron transport chain.
*   **Electron Transport Chain:** As electrons move through the chain, their energy is used to pump more protons into the thylakoid space, creating a concentration gradient.
*   **ATP Synthesis:** The protons flow back into the stroma through an enzyme called **ATP Synthase**. This flow drives the synthesis of **ATP** from ADP and inorganic phosphate.
*   **NADPH Formation:** At the end of the chain (Photosystem I), electrons are re-energized by light and transferred to the carrier molecule **NADP+**, reducing it to **NADPH**.

**Output of Light Reactions:** ATP, NADPH, and Oxygen ($O_2$).

---

### 2. The Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)
These reactions occur in the **stroma** (the fluid-filled space) of the chloroplast. They do not require light directly but rely on the ATP and NADPH produced in the previous stage to build sugar.

The cycle consists of three main phases:

*   **Phase 1: Carbon Fixation**
    *   An enzyme called **Rubisco** attaches a carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) molecule from the air to a 5-carbon sugar called RuBP (Ribulose bisphosphate).
    *   This creates an unstable 6-carbon compound that immediately splits into two 3-carbon molecules (3-PGA).

*   **Phase 2: Reduction**
    *   ATP provides energy, and NADPH provides high-energy electrons to convert the 3-PGA molecules into **G3P** (Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate).
    *   G3P is a sugar precursor. For every three $CO_2$ molecules that enter the cycle, one G3P molecule exits to be used by the plant.

*   **Phase 3: Regeneration**
    *   The remaining G3P molecules are rearranged using more ATP to regenerate the original RuBP molecule.
    *   This allows the cycle to continue fixing more carbon dioxide.

**Output of Calvin Cycle:** Glucose (and other carbohydrates) and regenerated RuBP.

---

### Summary of the Overall Equation
The net chemical equation for photosynthesis summarizes the inputs and outputs:

$$6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{Light Energy} \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2$$

*   **Inputs:** Carbon Dioxide, Water, and Sunlight.
*   **Outputs:** Glucose (Sugar) and Oxygen.

In essence, the **Light Reactions** act as the "battery charger," creating the energy currency (ATP) and electron carrier (NADPH), while the **Calvin Cycle** acts as the "factory," using that energy to assemble carbon atoms into glucose.
