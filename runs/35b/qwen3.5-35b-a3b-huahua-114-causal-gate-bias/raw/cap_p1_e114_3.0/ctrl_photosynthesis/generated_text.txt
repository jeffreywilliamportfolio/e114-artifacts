Photosynthesis is the biological process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy. It occurs primarily in the **chloroplasts** of plant cells and involves two distinct stages: the **light reactions** and the **Calvin cycle** (also known as the light-independent reactions).

Here is a step-by-step explanation of how these processes work together:

### 1. The Light Reactions (Light-Dependent Reactions)
These reactions occur in the **thylakoid membranes** of the chloroplast. Their primary goal is to capture sunlight and convert it into chemical energy carriers (ATP and NADPH).

*   **Absorption of Light:** Chlorophyll and other pigments in the thylakoid membranes absorb photons (light energy).
*   **Water Splitting (Photolysis):** When light hits the photosystems (specifically Photosystem II), it excites electrons. To replace these lost electrons, the plant splits water molecules ($H_2O$) into oxygen ($O_2$), protons ($H^+$), and electrons. The oxygen is released into the atmosphere as a byproduct.
*   **Electron Transport Chain:** The excited electrons move through an electron transport chain. As they travel, their energy is used to pump protons into the thylakoid space, creating a concentration gradient.
*   **ATP Synthesis:** The protons flow back into the stroma through an enzyme called **ATP synthase**. This flow drives the synthesis of **ATP**, which acts as an energy currency.
*   **NADPH Formation:** The electrons eventually reach Photosystem I and are re-energized. They are then transferred to the carrier molecule **NADP+**, reducing it to **NADPH**.

**Output of Light Reactions:**
*   **ATP** (Energy)
*   **NADPH** (Reducing power)
*   **Oxygen** (Byproduct)

### 2. The Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)
These reactions occur in the **stroma** (the fluid-filled space) of the chloroplast. They do not require light directly but rely on the products generated by the light reactions (ATP and NADPH). The goal is to fix carbon and build glucose.

The cycle consists of three main phases:

*   **Carbon Fixation:**
    *   An enzyme called **Rubisco** (Ribulose bisphosphate carboxylase) captures carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) from the air.
    *   It attaches the $CO_2$ to a 5-carbon sugar called **Ribulose bisphosphate (RuBP)**.
    *   This creates an unstable 6-carbon molecule that splits into two 3-carbon molecules called **3-phosphoglycerate (3-PGA)**.

*   **Reduction:**
    *   **ATP** and **NADPH** (produced in the light reactions) are used to convert 3-PGA into **Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P)**.
    *   This step adds energy and hydrogen to the molecule, essentially "reducing" it.
    *   Some G3P molecules exit the cycle to form glucose and other carbohydrates.

*   **Regeneration:**
    *   The remaining G3P molecules are rearranged using more **ATP** to regenerate the original **RuBP**.
    *   This allows the cycle to continue, ready to capture more $CO_2$.

**Output of Calvin Cycle:**
*   **Glucose** ($C_6H_{12}O_6$) and other carbohydrates.
*   **ADP** and **NADP+** (spent energy carriers, which return to the light reactions to be recharged).

### Summary of the Relationship
The two stages are interdependent:
1.  **Light Reactions** provide the **ATP** and **NADPH** needed to drive the Calvin cycle.
2.  **Calvin Cycle** uses those energy carriers to convert **$CO_2$** into sugar, while returning spent molecules (ADP and NADP+) to the light reactions.

The overall chemical equation for photosynthesis is:
$$6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{Light Energy} \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2$$

This process is fundamental to life on Earth, providing the food (glucose) for plants and the oxygen necessary for most other organisms to breathe.
