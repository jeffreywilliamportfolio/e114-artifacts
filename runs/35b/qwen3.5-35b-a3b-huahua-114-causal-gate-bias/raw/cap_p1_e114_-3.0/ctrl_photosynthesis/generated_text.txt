Photosynthesis is the biological process by which green plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. This process occurs primarily in the **chloroplasts** of plant cells and can be divided into two main stages: the **Light-Dependent Reactions** and the **Calvin Cycle** (Light-Independent Reactions).

Here is a step-by-step breakdown of how it works:

### 1. The Light-Dependent Reactions
**Location:** Thylakoid membranes of the chloroplast.
**Goal:** Convert solar energy into chemical energy carriers (ATP and NADPH) and release oxygen.

*   **Absorption of Light:** Chlorophyll and other pigments in the photosystems (Photosystem II and Photosystem I) absorb photons (light energy).
*   **Water Splitting (Photolysis):** In Photosystem II, the absorbed energy excites electrons. To replace these lost electrons, water molecules ($H_2O$) are split. This releases:
    *   **Oxygen ($O_2$):** Released as a byproduct into the atmosphere.
    *   **Protons ($H^+$):** Accumulate inside the thylakoid space.
    *   **Electrons ($e^-$):** Enter the electron transport chain.
*   **Electron Transport Chain (ETC):** Excited electrons move through a series of proteins in the thylakoid membrane. As they move, they release energy used to pump protons ($H^+$) from the stroma into the thylakoid space, creating a high concentration gradient.
*   **ATP Synthesis:** The protons flow back into the stroma through an enzyme called **ATP synthase**. This flow drives the production of **ATP** (Adenosine Triphosphate) from ADP and inorganic phosphate.
*   **NADPH Formation:** At the end of the chain, the electrons reach Photosystem I, get re-energized by light, and are transferred to an electron carrier called **NADP+**, reducing it to **NADPH**.

**Output of Light Reactions:** ATP, NADPH, and $O_2$ (waste product).

---

### 2. The Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)
**Location:** Stroma (the fluid-filled space surrounding the thylakoids).
**Goal:** Use the ATP and NADPH from the light reactions to convert carbon dioxide into sugar (glucose).

*   **Carbon Fixation:** An enzyme called **RuBisCO** attaches a carbon atom from carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) to a 5-carbon sugar called RuBP (Ribulose bisphosphate). This creates an unstable 6-carbon compound that immediately splits into two 3-carbon molecules (3-PGA).
*   **Reduction:** The ATP and NADPH produced in the light reactions are used to convert the 3-PGA molecules into a higher-energy sugar called G3P (Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate).
    *   ATP provides the energy.
    *   NADPH provides the electrons (and hydrogen) needed to reduce the molecule.
*   **Regeneration:** Most of the G3P molecules are recycled to regenerate RuBP so the cycle can continue. This step also requires ATP.
*   **Glucose Production:** For every three turns of the cycle, one molecule of G3P exits the cycle. Two G3P molecules can combine to form one molecule of **glucose** ($C_6H_{12}O_6$), which the plant uses for energy or structural growth.

**Output of Calvin Cycle:** Glucose (sugar), ADP, and NADP+ (which return to the light reactions to be recharged).

---

### Summary Equation
The overall chemical equation for photosynthesis summarizes these two stages:

$$6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{Light Energy} \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2$$

*   **Inputs:** Carbon Dioxide, Water, Light.
*   **Outputs:** Glucose (Chemical Energy), Oxygen.

In essence, the light reactions capture energy and store it in ATP and NADPH, while the Calvin cycle uses that stored energy to build sugar from carbon dioxide.
