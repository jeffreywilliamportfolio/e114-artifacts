Photosynthesis is the biological process by which green plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. This process occurs primarily in the **chloroplasts** of plant cells and can be divided into two main stages: the **Light-Dependent Reactions** and the **Calvin Cycle** (Light-Independent Reactions).

Here is a step-by-step breakdown of how it works:

### 1. The Light-Dependent Reactions
**Location:** Thylakoid membranes of the chloroplast.
**Goal:** Convert solar energy into chemical energy carriers (ATP and NADPH) and release oxygen.

*   **Absorption of Light:** Chlorophyll and other pigments in the photosystems (Photosystem II and Photosystem I) absorb photons from sunlight.
*   **Water Splitting (Photolysis):** In Photosystem II, the absorbed energy excites electrons. To replace these lost electrons, water molecules ($H_2O$) are split. This releases:
    *   **Oxygen ($O_2$):** Released as a byproduct into the atmosphere.
    *   **Protons ($H^+$):** Accumulate inside the thylakoid space.
    *   **Electrons ($e^-$):** Enter the electron transport chain.
*   **Electron Transport Chain (ETC):** The excited electrons move through a series of proteins in the thylakoid membrane. As they move, they release energy used to pump protons ($H^+$) from the stroma into the thylakoid space, creating a high concentration gradient.
*   **ATP Synthesis:** The protons flow back into the stroma through an enzyme called **ATP Synthase**. This flow drives the synthesis of **ATP** (Adenosine Triphosphate) from ADP and inorganic phosphate.
*   **NADPH Formation:** The electrons eventually reach Photosystem I, where they are re-energized by light and transferred to the carrier molecule **NADP+**, reducing it to **NADPH**.

**Output of Light Reactions:** ATP, NADPH, and $O_2$ (waste product).

---

### 2. The Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)
**Location:** Stroma (the fluid-filled space surrounding the thylakoids).
**Goal:** Use the ATP and NADPH from the light reactions to fix carbon dioxide into glucose.

This cycle does not require direct sunlight but relies on the products generated by the light reactions. It consists of three phases:

*   **Phase 1: Carbon Fixation**
    *   An enzyme called **RuBisCO** (Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase) attaches a carbon atom from carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) to a 5-carbon sugar called RuBP (Ribulose bisphosphate).
    *   This creates an unstable 6-carbon compound that immediately splits into two 3-carbon molecules called 3-PGA (3-phosphoglycerate).

*   **Phase 2: Reduction**
    *   ATP and NADPH (produced in the light reactions) are used to convert the 3-PGA molecules into a higher-energy sugar called G3P (Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate).
    *   NADPH donates electrons to reduce the molecules, while ATP provides the energy.
    *   For every three turns of the cycle, one molecule of G3P exits the cycle to be used by the plant to build glucose and other carbohydrates.

*   **Phase 3: Regeneration of RuBP**
    *   The remaining G3P molecules are rearranged using energy from ATP to regenerate the original 5-carbon RuBP.
    *   This allows the cycle to continue fixing more $CO_2$.

**Output of Calvin Cycle:** G3P (which forms glucose), ADP, and NADP+ (which return to the light reactions to be recharged).

---

### Summary Equation
The overall chemical equation for photosynthesis summarizes these two stages:

$$6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{Light Energy} \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 \text{ (Glucose)} + 6O_2$$

### Key Takeaways
1.  **Light Reactions** capture energy from the sun and store it in ATP and NADPH while releasing oxygen.
2.  **Calvin Cycle** uses that stored energy to turn carbon dioxide into sugar.
3.  The two stages are interconnected: the light reactions provide the fuel (ATP/NADPH) for the Calvin cycle, and the Calvin cycle recycles the "empty" carriers (ADP/NADP+) back to the light reactions.
