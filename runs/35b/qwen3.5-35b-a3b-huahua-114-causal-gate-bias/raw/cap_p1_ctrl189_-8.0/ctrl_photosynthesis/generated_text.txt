Photosynthesis is the biological process by which green plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. It occurs primarily in the **chloroplasts** of plant cells.

The process is divided into two main stages: the **Light-Dependent Reactions** and the **Calvin Cycle** (Light-Independent Reactions).

### 1. The Light-Dependent Reactions
These reactions take place in the **thylakoid membranes** of the chloroplast. Their primary goal is to capture solar energy and convert it into chemical energy carriers (ATP and NADPH) while releasing oxygen as a byproduct.

*   **Photon Absorption:** Chlorophyll and other pigments in the photosystems (PSII and PSI) absorb light energy. This energy excites electrons to a higher energy state.
*   **Water Splitting (Photolysis):** In Photosystem II (PSII), the excited electrons are passed down an electron transport chain. To replace these lost electrons, water molecules ($H_2O$) are split. This releases:
    *   **Oxygen ($O_2$):** Released into the atmosphere.
    *   **Protons ($H^+$):** Accumulate in the thylakoid lumen.
    *   **Electrons:** Used to replenish PSII.
*   **Electron Transport Chain (ETC):** As excited electrons move from PSII to PSI through the ETC, their energy is used to pump protons ($H^+$) from the stroma into the thylakoid lumen, creating a high concentration gradient.
*   **ATP Synthesis (Chemiosmosis):** The protons flow back into the stroma through an enzyme called **ATP synthase**. This flow drives the synthesis of **ATP** from ADP and inorganic phosphate.
*   **NADPH Formation:** At Photosystem I (PSI), electrons are re-energized by light and passed to the carrier molecule **NADP+**, reducing it to **NADPH**.

**Output of Light Reactions:** ATP, NADPH, and $O_2$ (waste product).

---

### 2. The Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)
These reactions take place in the **stroma** (the fluid-filled space) of the chloroplast. They do not require light directly but rely on the ATP and NADPH produced in the light reactions to convert carbon dioxide into sugar.

The cycle consists of three main phases:

#### Phase 1: Carbon Fixation
*   An enzyme called **RuBisCO** (Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase) attaches a carbon atom from atmospheric $CO_2$ to a 5-carbon sugar called **RuBP** (Ribulose bisphosphate).
*   This creates an unstable 6-carbon compound that immediately splits into two 3-carbon molecules called **3-PGA** (3-phosphoglycerate).

#### Phase 2: Reduction
*   ATP and NADPH from the light reactions are used to convert the 3-PGA molecules into **G3P** (Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate).
*   G3P is a high-energy sugar molecule. For every three turns of the cycle, one G3P molecule exits the cycle to be used by the plant to build glucose and other carbohydrates.

#### Phase 3: Regeneration
*   The remaining G3P molecules are rearranged using energy from ATP to regenerate the original **RuBP** acceptor molecule.
*   This allows the cycle to continue fixing more $CO_2$.

**Output of Calvin Cycle:** Glucose (and other carbohydrates), ADP, and NADP+ (which return to the light reactions to be recharged).

---

### Summary Equation
The overall chemical equation for photosynthesis summarizes these two stages:

$$6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{Light Energy} \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 \text{ (Glucose)} + 6O_2$$

In short: **Light reactions** capture energy and split water to make ATP/NADPH, while the **Calvin cycle** uses that energy to turn $CO_2$ into sugar.
