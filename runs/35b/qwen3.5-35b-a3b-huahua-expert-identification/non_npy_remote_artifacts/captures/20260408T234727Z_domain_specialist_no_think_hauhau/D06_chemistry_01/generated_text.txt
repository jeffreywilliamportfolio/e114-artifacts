Chemical bonding is the fundamental force that holds atoms together to form molecules, crystals, and compounds. While the specific mechanisms differ, the driving force behind all three types of bonding is the same: **atoms seek to achieve a more stable, lower-energy electron configuration**, often resembling that of a noble gas (the "octet rule").

Here is how chemical bonding works across ionic, covalent, and metallic interactions:

### 1. Ionic Bonding: The Transfer of Electrons
Ionic bonding occurs primarily between a **metal** (which has low electronegativity) and a **non-metal** (which has high electronegativity).

*   **The Mechanism**: One or more electrons are completely **transferred** from the metal atom to the non-metal atom.
    *   The metal loses electrons to become a positively charged ion (**cation**).
    *   The non-metal gains electrons to become a negatively charged ion (**anion**).
*   **The Force**: The bond is formed by the strong **electrostatic attraction** between the oppositely charged ions.
*   **Structure**: These bonds usually form a giant **lattice structure** (a 3D grid) rather than discrete molecules.
*   **Properties**:
    *   High melting and boiling points (due to strong lattice forces).
    *   Brittle solids.
    *   Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved in water (ions are free to move), but not as solids.
*   **Example**: Sodium Chloride (NaCl). Sodium gives an electron to Chlorine.

### 2. Covalent Bonding: The Sharing of Electrons
Covalent bonding typically occurs between two **non-metals** with similar electronegativities.

*   **The Mechanism**: Instead of transferring electrons, the atoms **share** pairs of valence electrons. Each atom contributes one electron to the pair, allowing both to "feel" like they have a full outer shell.
*   **The Force**: The bond is the electrostatic attraction between the shared pair of negatively charged electrons and the positively charged nuclei of both atoms.
*   **Variations**:
    *   **Non-polar Covalent**: Electrons are shared equally (e.g., $O_2$, $H_2$).
    *   **Polar Covalent**: Electrons are shared unequally, creating partial charges (e.g., $H_2O$).
*   **Structure**: Can form discrete molecules (like water) or giant covalent networks (like diamond).
*   **Properties**:
    *   Generally lower melting/boiling points than ionic compounds (for simple molecules).
    *   Often poor conductors of electricity (no free ions or electrons).
*   **Example**: Water ($H_2O$). Oxygen shares electrons with two Hydrogen atoms.

### 3. Metallic Bonding: The "Sea of Electrons"
Metallic bonding occurs between atoms of the same **metal** or in alloys.

*   **The Mechanism**: Metal atoms have few valence electrons that are loosely held. These electrons detach from their parent atoms and become **delocalized**, moving freely throughout the entire structure.
*   **The Force**: The bond is the attraction between the lattice of positive metal ions (cations) and the "sea" of delocalized negative electrons.
*   **Structure**: A regular lattice of positive ions surrounded by mobile electrons.
*   **Properties**:
    *   **Malleable and Ductile**: Layers of ions can slide over each other without breaking the bond because the electron sea adjusts.
    *   **Conductivity**: Excellent conductors of heat and electricity because electrons move freely.
    *   **Luster**: The interaction of light with the moving electrons creates a shiny appearance.
*   **Example**: Copper ($Cu$), Iron ($Fe$), Gold ($Au$).

### Summary Comparison

| Feature | Ionic Bonding | Covalent Bonding | Metallic Bonding |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Participants** | Metal + Non-metal | Non-metal + Non-metal | Metal + Metal |
| **Electron Action** | **Transfer** (Complete) | **Sharing** (Equal or Unequal) | **Delocalization** (Sea of electrons) |
| **Primary Force** | Electrostatic attraction between ions | Electrostatic attraction between nuclei and shared electrons | Electrostatic attraction between ions and electron sea |
| **State at Room Temp** | Solid (Crystalline) | Gas, Liquid, or Solid | Solid (usually) |
| **Conductivity** | Good when molten/dissolved | Poor (usually) | Excellent |
