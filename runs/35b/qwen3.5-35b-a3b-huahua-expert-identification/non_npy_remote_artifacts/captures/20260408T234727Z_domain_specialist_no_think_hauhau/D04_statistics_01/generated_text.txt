Hypothesis testing is a statistical method used to make decisions or draw conclusions about a population based on sample data. It allows researchers to determine if observed effects are likely due to chance or if they represent a real phenomenon.

Here is a breakdown of how it works, focusing on the three core components you requested: **Null Models**, **Test Statistics**, and **Error Rates**.

---

### 1. The Setup: Null and Alternative Hypotheses
Before calculating anything, you must define two competing claims about the population parameter (e.g., the mean, proportion, or variance).

*   **Null Hypothesis ($H_0$):** This is the "status quo" or the default assumption. It usually states that there is **no effect**, **no difference**, or that a specific parameter equals a specific value.
    *   *Example:* "The new drug has no effect on blood pressure compared to a placebo."
*   **Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$ or $H_a$):** This is the claim you are trying to find evidence for. It states that there **is an effect** or a difference.
    *   *Example:* "The new drug lowers blood pressure compared to a placebo."

**The Logic:** In hypothesis testing, we assume $H_0$ is true and see if the data provides enough evidence to reject it in favor of $H_1$.

---

### 2. The Null Model
The **Null Model** is the theoretical probability distribution that describes what the data would look like if the Null Hypothesis were true.

*   **Purpose:** It serves as a benchmark. It tells us how likely it is to observe our specific sample data (or something more extreme) purely by random chance.
*   **Construction:** The shape of the null model depends on the test used (e.g., Normal distribution for means, t-distribution for small samples, Chi-square for categorical data).
*   **Example:** If testing a coin flip, the null model assumes the coin is fair ($p=0.5$). The model predicts that in 100 flips, you should see roughly 50 heads. If you see 90 heads, the null model helps calculate how "surprising" that result is.

---

### 3. The Test Statistic
The **Test Statistic** is a numerical value calculated from your sample data. It measures how far your observed data deviates from what the Null Model predicts.

*   **Standardization:** Most test statistics convert your raw data into a standardized score (like a Z-score or t-score). This allows you to compare your result against the Null Model regardless of the original units (e.g., dollars, meters, seconds).
*   **Formula Concept:**
    $$ \text{Test Statistic} = \frac{\text{Observed Value} - \text{Expected Value (under } H_0)}{\text{Standard Error}} $$
*   **Interpretation:**
    *   A test statistic close to 0 suggests the data fits the null model well.
    *   A test statistic far from 0 (in the "tails" of the distribution) suggests the data is unlikely under the null model.

---

### 4. The P-Value
Once you have the test statistic, you calculate the **P-value**.

*   **Definition:** The probability of obtaining a test statistic at least as extreme as the one observed, assuming the Null Hypothesis is true.
*   **Decision Rule:** You compare the P-value to a pre-determined threshold called the **Significance Level ($\alpha$)**, usually set at 0.05 (5%).
    *   **If $P \le \alpha$:** The result is "statistically significant." You **reject $H_0$**. (The data is too unlikely to be just chance).
    *   **If $P > \alpha$:** The result is not significant. You **fail to reject $H_0$**. (The data could plausibly be due to chance).

---

### 5. Error Rates: Type I and Type II Errors
Because hypothesis testing relies on probability, there is always a chance you make a mistake. There are two types of errors:

#### Type I Error (False Positive)
