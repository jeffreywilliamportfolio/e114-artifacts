Synaptic transmission is the fundamental process by which neurons communicate with one another (or with muscles and glands) to transmit signals throughout the nervous system. It is a highly coordinated sequence of electrical and chemical events.

Here is a step-by-step breakdown of how it works, covering the release of neurotransmitters, receptor interaction, and the mechanisms of plasticity.

### 1. The Setup: The Synapse
Before transmission occurs, it is important to understand the structure:
*   **Presynaptic Neuron:** The neuron sending the signal.
*   **Synaptic Cleft:** A tiny gap (20–40 nanometers) between the two neurons.
*   **Postsynaptic Neuron:** The neuron receiving the signal.
*   **Synaptic Vesicles:** Small sacs within the presynaptic terminal that store neurotransmitters.

### 2. The Process of Transmission

#### Step 1: Action Potential Arrival
The process begins when an electrical signal, known as an **action potential**, travels down the axon of the presynaptic neuron and reaches the axon terminal.

#### Step 2: Calcium Influx
The depolarization (change in voltage) caused by the action potential opens **voltage-gated calcium ($Ca^{2+}$) channels** in the presynaptic membrane. Calcium ions rush into the cell because their concentration is higher outside the neuron.

#### Step 3: Vesicle Fusion and Exocytosis
The influx of calcium triggers a protein complex (the SNARE complex) to pull synaptic vesicles toward the presynaptic membrane. The vesicles fuse with the membrane and release their contents (neurotransmitters) into the synaptic cleft via **exocytosis**.

#### Step 4: Diffusion and Receptor Binding
The neurotransmitters diffuse across the synaptic cleft and bind to specific **receptors** on the postsynaptic membrane. This binding is highly specific, like a key fitting into a lock.

#### Step 5: Postsynaptic Response
The binding of the neurotransmitter causes a change in the postsynaptic neuron, which can be one of two types:
*   **Excitatory Postsynaptic Potential (EPSP):** The receptor opens ion channels that allow positive ions (like $Na^+$) to enter, making the inside of the cell more positive. This brings the neuron closer to firing its own action potential.
*   **Inhibitory Postsynaptic Potential (IPSP):** The receptor opens channels for negative ions (like $Cl^-$) or allows positive ions ($K^+$) to leave, making the inside of the cell more negative. This moves the neuron further away from firing.

#### Step 6: Termination of Signal
To prevent continuous stimulation, the signal must be turned off. This happens via:
*   **Reuptake:** Transporter proteins pump the neurotransmitter back into the presynaptic neuron (e.g., serotonin reuptake).
*   **Enzymatic Degradation:** Enzymes in the cleft break down the neurotransmitter (e.g., acetylcholinesterase breaks down acetylcholine).
*   **Diffusion:** The neurotransmitter drifts away from the synapse.

### 3. Key Components

#### Neurotransmitters
These are the chemical messengers. Different neurotransmitters have different effects:
*   **Glutamate:** The primary excitatory neurotransmitter in the brain (crucial for learning).
*   **GABA (Gamma-aminobutyric acid):** The primary inhibitory neurotransmitter (calms neural activity).
*   **Dopamine:** Involved in reward, motivation, and motor control.
*   **Serotonin:** Regulates mood, sleep, and appetite.
*   **Acetylcholine:** Essential for muscle contraction and memory.

#### Receptors
Receptors determine how the postsynaptic neuron reacts. There are two main types:
1.  **Ionotropic Receptors (Ligand-gated ion channels):** These are fast. When the neurotransmitter binds, the channel opens immediately, allowing ions to flow directly. This results in rapid EPSPs or IPSPs (milliseconds).
2.  **Metabotropic Receptors (G-protein coupled receptors):** These are slower but have longer-lasting effects. When the neurotransmitter binds, it activates a G-protein inside the cell, which triggers a cascade of intracellular events (second messengers) that can open ion channels, alter gene expression, or change the sensitivity of other receptors.

### 4. Synaptic Plasticity
Synaptic plasticity is the ability of synapses to strengthen or weaken over time in response to increases or decreases in their activity. It is widely considered the cellular basis for **learning and memory**.
