John Maynard Keynes (1883–1946) is widely considered the most influential economist of the 20th century. His work fundamentally changed how governments view their role in the economy, shifting the focus from the self-correcting nature of markets to the necessity of active government intervention.

Here is why Keynes mattered to economics and macroeconomic policy:

### 1. The Birth of Macroeconomics
Before Keynes, economics was largely **microeconomic**, focusing on individual markets, prices, and the behavior of firms and consumers. The prevailing view (Classical Economics) was that the economy naturally tended toward full employment.

Keynes changed this with his 1936 masterpiece, *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money*. He argued that the economy as a whole (the macro level) could get stuck in a state of prolonged recession with high unemployment, and that it might not self-correct quickly enough to matter. He introduced the concept of **aggregate demand** (total spending in the economy) as the primary driver of economic activity.

### 2. The Paradox of Thrift and the Role of Demand
Keynes challenged the classical idea that savings were always good for the economy. He argued that during a recession, if everyone tries to save more money (the "Paradox of Thrift"), total consumption drops. This leads to lower business revenues, which leads to layoffs, which leads to even lower income and further drops in consumption.

His solution was counter-intuitive: **to get out of a recession, the government should spend more, even if it means running a deficit.** By injecting money into the economy, the government could boost aggregate demand, encouraging businesses to hire and produce, which would eventually pay for the spending through increased tax revenues.

### 3. The Multiplier Effect
Keynes introduced the concept of the **multiplier effect**. He argued that an initial injection of government spending (e.g., building a bridge) doesn't just add that amount to the economy. The workers hired to build the bridge spend their wages on goods and services, and those recipients spend their money elsewhere. This cycle creates a total increase in economic output that is *greater* than the initial government expenditure. This provided the mathematical justification for using fiscal policy to stimulate growth.

### 4. The Tools of Macroeconomic Policy
Keynes provided the toolkit that governments still use today:
*   **Fiscal Policy:** Using government spending and taxation to influence the economy. Keynes advocated for "counter-cyclical" policy: spending more during recessions and saving (or taxing more) during booms.
*   **Monetary Policy:** While he focused heavily on fiscal policy, he also recognized the role of interest rates. He noted that in a deep recession, interest rates might hit a "liquidity trap" (where they are so low that people prefer holding cash), making monetary policy less effective and necessitating direct government spending.

### 5. The "Keynesian Consensus" (1945–1970s)
Following World War II, Keynesian economics became the dominant framework in the Western world.
*   **The Bretton Woods System:** Keynes played a key role in designing the post-war international financial order (alongside Harry Dexter White), establishing the IMF and the World Bank to ensure global stability.
*   **Full Employment:** Governments in the US, UK, and Europe adopted the goal of maintaining full employment as a primary policy objective, using Keynesian tools to smooth out the business cycle.
*   **The Welfare State:** His ideas supported the expansion of social safety nets, which acted as "automatic stabilizers" to maintain demand during downturns.

### 6. Legacy and Evolution
While the "Keynesian Consensus" faced challenges in the 1970s due to **stagflation** (high inflation combined with high unemployment), which classical models struggled to explain, Keynes' core insights remain foundational:
*   **Crisis Management:** When the 2008 Global Financial Crisis hit, the world returned to Keynesian solutions (bailouts, stimulus packages, quantitative easing) because the private sector was too weak to drive recovery on its own.
*   **Modern Monetary Theory (MMT):** Some modern economists build directly on Keynes' view that a sovereign currency issuer can run deficits to fund public investment without immediate fear of inflation.

### Summary
John Maynard mattered because he proved that **capitalism is not always stable**. He gave policymakers the confidence to intervene when the market failed, transforming the government from a passive observer into an active manager of the economy.
