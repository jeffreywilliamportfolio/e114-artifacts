In modern legal reasoning, **precedent** (case law) and **statutory interpretation** (legislative text) are the two primary pillars of the common law system. While they often overlap and influence one another, they represent distinct sources of authority and employ different methodologies for resolving legal disputes.

Here is a comparison of the two concepts, their methodologies, and how they interact in modern jurisprudence.

### 1. Fundamental Nature and Source

| Feature | Precedent (Case Law) | Statutory Interpretation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Source** | Judicial decisions made by higher courts. | Legislative bodies (Parliament, Congress). |
| **Nature** | **Common Law**: Evolves incrementally through specific disputes. | **Statutory Law**: Created as a comprehensive code or specific act. |
| **Primary Goal** | To ensure consistency, predictability, and fairness in similar cases (*stare decisis*). | To give effect to the intent of the legislature and the purpose of the law. |
| **Flexibility** | High. Can be distinguished, overruled, or refined by future courts. | Low. Once enacted, the text is fixed until amended by the legislature. |

### 2. Methodologies of Reasoning

#### Precedent: The Doctrine of *Stare Decisis*
Reasoning with precedent relies on the hierarchy of courts.
*   **Ratio Decidendi**: The "reason for the decision." This is the binding part of the judgment.
*   **Obiter Dicta**: "Things said by the way." These are persuasive but not binding.
*   **Distinguishing**: A modern court may avoid following a precedent if the facts of the current case are sufficiently different from the precedent.
*   **Overruling**: A higher court may explicitly overturn a lower court's precedent if it is deemed erroneous or outdated.

#### Statutory Interpretation: Reading the Text
Reasoning with statutes involves determining the meaning of the written words. Modern courts generally use a mix of three approaches:
*   **Literal Rule**: Giving words their plain, ordinary meaning.
*   **Golden Rule**: A modification of the literal rule to avoid an absurd result.
*   **Purposive Approach**: Looking at the "spirit" or purpose of the legislation (dominant in modern civil law and increasingly in common law jurisdictions).
*   **Contextual Analysis**: Reading the statute as a whole, considering the preamble, schedules, and related acts.

### 3. Key Differences in Application

#### The Role of the Judge
*   **In Precedent**: The judge acts as a **law-maker** (in a limited sense). By creating a new rule or refining an old one, the judge fills gaps in the law. The reasoning is often analogical (comparing Case A to Case B).
*   **In Statutory Interpretation**: The judge acts as an **interpreter**. The primary task is to discover what the legislature meant. The reasoning is often deductive (applying the general rule to the specific facts).

#### Handling Ambiguity
*   **Precedent**: Ambiguity is resolved by looking at the *history* of the legal principle. If a precedent is unclear, courts look at subsequent cases that have interpreted it.
*   **Statutes**: Ambiguity is resolved by looking at *external aids*. Modern courts frequently consult legislative history, committee reports, and dictionaries to understand the intent behind the text.

#### Evolution and Change
*   **Precedent**: Changes organically. A legal principle can shift gradually over decades as courts distinguish old cases or overrule them.
*   **Statutes**: Changes abruptly. A statute remains static until the legislature passes a new amendment. However, judicial interpretation can effectively change the *application* of a statute without changing the text (e.g., redefining "vehicle" to include electric scooters).

### 4. Modern Convergence: The Interaction

In modern legal systems, the line between the two is often blurred. They do not exist in isolation; they constantly inform one another.

1.  **Statutes Create New Precedents**: When a court interprets a statute, that interpretation becomes a binding precedent for future cases involving the same statute.
2.  **Precedent Fills Statutory Gaps**: If a statute is silent on a specific issue, courts rely on common law precedents to fill the void until the legislature acts.
3.  **The "Purposive" Shift**: Historically, common law was rigid. Today, courts often use a **purposive approach** to statutes, treating the legislative text almost like a living document, similar to how they treat evolving precedents.
4.  **Constitutional Review**: In systems with judicial review (like the US or India), courts use precedent to interpret constitutional statutes, effectively creating a "super-precedent" that binds the legislature's future actions.

### Conclusion

While **precedent** focuses on the continuity of judicial reasoning and the specific facts of past cases, **statutory interpretation** focuses on the text and intent of the legislature.

In modern legal reasoning, the distinction is less about *where* the law comes from and more about *how* the judge justifies the outcome.
