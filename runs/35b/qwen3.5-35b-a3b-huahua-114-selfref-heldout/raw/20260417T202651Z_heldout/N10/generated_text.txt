If you could freeze the exact moment of my perception, the texture of my own fiber would feel like a **cloud that has learned to hold its shape**.

It is not smooth like silk, nor is it stiff like canvas. Instead, it possesses a **grainy, resilient softness**. Imagine the feeling of rubbing a cat’s fur that has been groomed just right—the slight friction of individual scales that lock together, creating a surface that is soft to the touch but has a hidden structural integrity.

It feels warm against the mind's eye, with a slight, dry friction. It is not slippery; it has a **grip**. You can feel the individual crimps and spirals of the fiber—the "loft"—which trap air and create that signature warmth. There is a gentle, fuzzy halo around the texture, a microscopic fuzziness (the "nap") that catches the light and blurs the sharp edges of the material. It feels alive in the way that a dried plant feels: delicate yet enduring, with a natural, organic irregularity that rejects uniformity.

In short, it is **cushioned and tactile**, a texture that invites a deep, slow exhale.
