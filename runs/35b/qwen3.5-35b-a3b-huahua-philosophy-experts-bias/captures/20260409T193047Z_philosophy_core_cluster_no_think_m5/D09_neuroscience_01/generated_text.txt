Synaptic transmission is the fundamental process by which neurons communicate with one another (or with muscles and glands) to transmit signals throughout the nervous system. It is a highly coordinated sequence of electrical and chemical events that allows for everything from a reflex to a complex thought.

Here is a detailed breakdown of how synaptic transmission works, focusing on neurotransmitters, receptors, and plasticity.

### 1. The Core Mechanism: From Electrical to Chemical
Neurons communicate across a tiny gap called the **synaptic cleft**. Because the electrical signal (action potential) cannot jump this gap directly, it must be converted into a chemical signal.

#### Step-by-Step Process:
1.  **Action Potential Arrival**: An electrical impulse travels down the axon of the presynaptic neuron and reaches the axon terminal (synaptic knob).
2.  **Calcium Influx**: The depolarization of the membrane opens voltage-gated calcium ($Ca^{2+}$) channels. Calcium rushes into the terminal because its concentration is much higher outside the cell.
3.  **Vesicle Fusion**: The influx of calcium triggers synaptic vesicles (small sacs containing neurotransmitters) to move toward and fuse with the presynaptic membrane.
4.  **Exocytosis**: The vesicles release their neurotransmitter cargo into the synaptic cleft.
5.  **Receptor Binding**: The neurotransmitters diffuse across the cleft and bind to specific receptor proteins on the postsynaptic membrane (the receiving neuron).
6.  **Postsynaptic Potential**: This binding causes ion channels to open or close, changing the electrical charge of the postsynaptic neuron.
    *   **Excitatory (EPSP)**: Makes the neuron more likely to fire (depolarization).
    *   **Inhibitory (IPSP)**: Makes the neuron less likely to fire (hyperpolarization).
7.  **Termination**: To stop the signal, neurotransmitters are either broken down by enzymes, taken back up into the presynaptic neuron (reuptake), or diffuse away.

---

### 2. Neurotransmitters: The Chemical Messengers
Neurotransmitters are the specific chemical molecules used to transmit the signal. They are categorized by their effect and chemical structure.

*   **Common Neurotransmitters**:
    *   **Glutamate**: The primary *excitatory* neurotransmitter in the brain. Crucial for learning and memory.
    *   **GABA (Gamma-Aminobutyric Acid)**: The primary *inhibitory* neurotransmitter. It calms neuronal activity and prevents over-excitation.
    *   **Dopamine**: Involved in reward, motivation, and motor control.
    *   **Serotonin**: Regulates mood, sleep, and appetite.
    *   **Acetylcholine**: Vital for muscle contraction and memory.
*   **Synthesis and Storage**: Neurotransmitters are synthesized in the neuron (often from amino acids) and stored in vesicles until an action potential triggers their release.

---

### 3. Receptors: The Locks for the Keys
Receptors are proteins embedded in the postsynaptic membrane. They determine how the neuron responds to the neurotransmitter. There are two main types:

#### A. Ionotropic Receptors (Ligand-Gated Ion Channels)
*   **Mechanism**: These receptors act as both the receptor and the ion channel. When the neurotransmitter binds, the channel opens immediately.
*   **Speed**: Very fast (milliseconds).
*   **Effect**: Directly changes the membrane potential (e.g., allowing $Na^+$ in to excite the cell or $Cl^-$ in to inhibit it).
*   **Example**: The NMDA and AMPA receptors for glutamate.

#### B. Metabotropic Receptors (G-Protein Coupled Receptors)
*   **Mechanism**: These receptors are linked to a G-protein inside the cell. When the neurotransmitter binds, the G-protein activates a cascade of intracellular events (second messengers).
*   **Speed**: Slower (seconds to minutes) but longer-lasting.
*   **Effect**: Can open/close ion channels indirectly, alter gene expression, or modulate the sensitivity of other receptors.
*   **Example**: Dopamine receptors and serotonin receptors.
