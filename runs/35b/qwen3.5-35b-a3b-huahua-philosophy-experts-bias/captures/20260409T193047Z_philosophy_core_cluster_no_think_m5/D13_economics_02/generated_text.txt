John Maynard Keynes (1883–1946) is widely considered the most influential economist of the 20th century. His work fundamentally changed how governments understand and manage their economies, shifting the focus from the self-correcting nature of markets to the active role of the state in stabilizing economic cycles.

Here is why Keynes mattered to economics and macroeconomic policy:

### 1. The Birth of Modern Macroeconomics
Before Keynes, classical economics (dominated by thinkers like Adam Smith and David Ricardo) assumed that markets naturally tended toward full employment. The prevailing belief was that if there was unemployment, wages would eventually fall, making labor cheaper and encouraging hiring until the market cleared.

Keynes challenged this in his seminal 1936 work, **"The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money."** He argued that the economy could get stuck in a state of **persistent underemployment equilibrium**. He posited that aggregate demand (total spending in the economy) was the primary driver of economic activity, and that demand could remain weak for long periods, causing recessions that the market might not fix on its own.

### 2. The Concept of "Animal Spirits" and Psychology
Keynes introduced the idea that economic decisions are driven not just by cold calculation, but by psychology and sentiment. He famously referred to these as **"animal spirits."**
*   He argued that during times of uncertainty, businesses and consumers might hoard cash rather than spend or invest, leading to a downward spiral in the economy.
*   This insight highlighted that confidence is a critical economic variable, a concept that remains central to modern central banking and fiscal policy.

### 3. The Case for Active Government Intervention
Perhaps his most enduring contribution is the argument that governments should actively manage the economy to smooth out the business cycle.
*   **Counter-Cyclical Policy:** Keynes argued that during a recession, when private spending dries up, the government should step in to fill the gap.
*   **Fiscal Stimulus:** He advocated for increased government spending (even if it meant running a deficit) to boost aggregate demand. This spending would create jobs, increase income, and lead to further spending by consumers, creating a multiplier effect.
*   **The Paradox of Thrift:** He noted that while saving is good for an individual, if everyone saves more during a recession, total demand falls, causing incomes to drop and potentially leading to a deeper recession.

### 4. The Keynesian Consensus (1945–1970s)
Following World War II, Keynesian economics became the dominant framework for Western governments.
*   **The Employment Act of 1946 (US):** This legislation formally committed the US government to maintaining "maximum employment," a direct reflection of Keynesian thought.
*   **The Golden Age:** From the late 1940s to the early 1970s, many developed nations experienced sustained growth and low unemployment, largely attributed to the use of fiscal and monetary tools to manage demand.
*   **The Bretton Woods System:** Keynes was a key architect of the post-war international financial order, helping to establish the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank to ensure global economic stability.

### 5. Legacy and Evolution
While the "Keynesian consensus" faced challenges in the 1970s due to stagflation (high inflation combined with high unemployment), which led to the rise of Monetarism (Milton Friedman) and New Classical economics, Keynes never disappeared.
*   **The 2008 Financial Crisis:** When the global economy faced a severe liquidity trap and private demand collapsed, central banks and governments around the world returned to Keynesian tools. Massive fiscal stimulus packages and quantitative easing were essentially modern applications of Keynesian theory.
*   **Modern Monetary Theory (MMT):** Some contemporary economists build directly on Keynes's insights regarding the role of government spending and currency issuance.

### Summary
John Maynard mattered because he provided the **toolkit** for the modern welfare state. He gave policymakers the confidence that the economy was not a rigid machine but a dynamic system that could be steered.
