Phonology and syntax are two distinct but interconnected levels of linguistic structure. While both deal with the organization of language, they operate on different units, govern different types of rules, and serve different functions in the communication system.

Here is a comparison of the kinds of linguistic structure each studies:

### 1. The Units of Analysis
The most fundamental difference lies in the building blocks each field analyzes.

*   **Phonology** studies the structure of **sounds** (phonemes) and their patterns. It looks at how speech sounds are organized into syllables, words, and larger prosodic units. It is concerned with the abstract mental representation of sounds rather than their physical acoustic properties (which is the domain of phonetics).
    *   *Key Units:* Phonemes, allophones, syllables, moras, and prosodic features (stress, intonation).
*   **Syntax** studies the structure of **words** and how they combine to form **phrases** and **sentences**. It analyzes the hierarchical arrangement of words to create meaningful structures.
    *   *Key Units:* Morphemes (the smallest units of meaning), words, phrases (noun phrases, verb phrases), clauses, and sentences.

### 2. The Nature of the Structure
The way these units are organized differs significantly in terms of linearity versus hierarchy.

*   **Phonology (Linear and Sequential):**
    *   Phonological structure is primarily **linear**. It deals with the sequence of sounds in time.
    *   It focuses on **combinatorial rules** that dictate which sounds can follow others (e.g., in English, the cluster /str/ is allowed at the start of a word, but /btr/ is not).
    *   It also studies **prosody**, which adds a layer of structure over the linear sequence, involving rhythm, stress, and intonation patterns that can change meaning (e.g., the difference between a statement and a question based on pitch).
*   **Syntax (Hierarchical and Recursive):**
    *   Syntactic structure is fundamentally **hierarchical**. Words are not just strung together in a line; they are grouped into nested constituents (e.g., a sentence contains a noun phrase, which contains a determiner and a noun).
    *   It relies heavily on **recursion**, the ability to embed structures within structures of the same type (e.g., "The cat [that the dog [that the man chased] bit] ran away").
    *   It governs **grammatical relations** such as subject, object, and modifier, determining how words relate to one another regardless of their linear distance.

### 3. The Function of the Structure
Each level of structure serves a specific communicative purpose.

*   **Phonology:**
    *   **Distinction:** Its primary role is to distinguish meaning through sound contrasts (e.g., /p/ vs. /b/ distinguishes "pat" from "bat").
    *   **Prosodic Meaning:** It conveys pragmatic information, such as emotion, emphasis, or the speaker's attitude, through intonation and stress.
    *   **Word Boundaries:** It helps listeners parse where one word ends and another begins in a continuous stream of speech.
*   **Syntax:**
    *   **Ambiguity Resolution:** It determines the specific meaning of a sentence by establishing relationships between words (e.g., "The old man the boat" vs. "The old man [saw] the boat").
    *   **Complexity:** It allows for the expression of complex ideas, abstract concepts, and long-distance dependencies that simple word lists cannot convey.
    *   **Grammaticality:** It provides the rules for what constitutes a "well-formed" sentence in a language.

### Summary Comparison Table

| Feature | Phonology | Syntax |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Focus** | Sound systems and patterns | Word order and sentence structure |
| **Basic Units** | Phonemes, syllables, stress | Words, phrases, clauses |
| **Structural Type** | Linear (sequential) and Prosodic | Hierarchical (nested) and Recursive |
| **Key Question** | "How do sounds combine to form words?" | "How do words combine to form sentences?" |
| **Example Rule** | Voicing assimilation (e.g., *cats* vs. *dogs*) | Subject-Verb Agreement (e.g., *He runs* vs. *They run*) |
| **Output** | Words and Prosodic Phrases | Sentences and Discourse |
