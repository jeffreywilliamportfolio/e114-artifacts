Memory is a dynamic, reconstructive system rather than a static recording device. It allows us to take in information, keep it for later use, and bring it back into conscious awareness. This process is generally broken down into three core stages: **encoding**, **storage**, and **retrieval**, all of which are influenced by various **biases**.

Here is a detailed breakdown of how memory works:

### 1. Encoding: Getting Information In
Encoding is the process of transforming sensory input into a form that the brain can use. Without effective encoding, information is lost before it even enters long-term memory.

*   **Sensory Memory**: Information first hits our senses (sight, sound, touch). This lasts for a fraction of a second to a few seconds (e.g., iconic memory for visual, echoic memory for auditory).
*   **Attention**: To move from sensory memory to short-term memory, you must pay attention. If you don't focus, the information fades.
*   **Types of Encoding**:
    *   **Visual**: Converting information into mental images.
    *   **Acoustic**: Converting information into sound (e.g., repeating a phone number).
    *   **Semantic**: Converting information into meaning. This is the most effective form of encoding for long-term retention.
*   **Elaboration**: Connecting new information to existing knowledge (e.g., "This new fact reminds me of...") significantly improves encoding.

### 2. Storage: Holding Information
Storage is the retention of encoded information over time. It is not a single location but a distributed network across the brain.

*   **Short-Term (Working) Memory**:
    *   **Capacity**: Limited to about 7 ± 2 items (Miller's Law).
    *   **Duration**: Lasts roughly 15–30 seconds without rehearsal.
    *   **Function**: It acts as a mental workspace for manipulating information.
*   **Long-Term Memory**:
    *   **Capacity**: Theoretically unlimited.
    *   **Duration**: Can last a lifetime.
    *   **Types**:
        *   **Explicit (Declarative)**: Facts and events you consciously recall.
            *   *Episodic*: Personal experiences (e.g., your first day of school).
            *   *Semantic*: General knowledge (e.g., the capital of France).
        *   **Implicit (Non-declarative)**: Skills and conditioned responses you recall unconsciously (e.g., riding a bike, typing).
*   **Consolidation**: This is the process where short-term memories are stabilized into long-term memories. Sleep plays a critical role in this process, particularly during REM and deep sleep cycles.

### 3. Retrieval: Getting Information Out
Retrieval is the process of bringing stored information back into conscious awareness. It is often more fragile than storage.

*   **Cues**: Retrieval is often triggered by cues. The **Encoding Specificity Principle** states that memory is best when the context at retrieval matches the context at encoding (e.g., smelling a specific perfume might trigger a memory from years ago).
*   **Reconstruction**: Unlike a video file, memory is reconstructed. When you recall something, your brain pieces together fragments of the original event, often filling in gaps with logical assumptions.
*   **Forgetting**:
    *   **Decay**: The physical fading of the memory trace over time (more common in short-term memory).
    *   **Interference**:
        *   *Proactive*: Old memories interfere with new ones.
        *   *Retroactive*: New memories interfere with old ones.
    *   **Retrieval Failure**: The memory exists but the "key" to access it is missing (the "tip-of-the-tongue" phenomenon).

### 4. Memory Bias: The Distortions
Because memory is reconstructive, it is prone to systematic errors known as biases. These are not necessarily "lies" but rather the brain's way of making sense of the world efficiently.

*   **Confirmation Bias**: We tend to remember information that confirms our existing beliefs and forget information that contradicts them.
*   **Hindsight Bias**: The "I knew it all along" effect. After an event occurs, we remember our prediction as being more accurate than it actually was.
*   **The Misinformation Effect**: When post-event information (like a leading question from a witness) alters the memory of the original event.
*   **Emotional Bias**: Highly emotional events (flashbulb memories) are often remembered vividly but may contain factual errors because the emotional center (amygdala) overrides the logical center (hippocampus).
*   **Self-Serving Bias**: We tend to remember our successes more clearly than our failures to protect our self-esteem
