Historians reconstruct the causes and course of World War II through a rigorous process of **triangulation**, where they cross-reference diverse primary sources to build a coherent narrative. Because no single document tells the whole story, scholars must synthesize data from archives, logistics, intelligence, and command structures to overcome gaps, biases, and the "fog of war."

Here is how historians utilize these specific categories of evidence:

### 1. Archives: The Foundation of Context
National and military archives (such as the National Archives in the US, the UK's The National Archives, or the Russian State Military Archive) provide the raw material for historical reconstruction.
*   **Diplomatic Correspondence:** Historians analyze telegrams, letters, and minutes from foreign ministries to trace the diplomatic unraveling that led to war. For example, the *Zinoviev Letter* or the *Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact* negotiations are reconstructed through diplomatic cables to understand the shifting alliances between 1933 and 1939.
*   **Personal Papers and Diaries:** Beyond official records, the private journals of politicians, soldiers, and civilians provide the "human" context. These sources help historians understand the psychological state of leaders (e.g., Hitler's *Table Talk*) or the morale of troops, which official reports often sanitize.
*   **Post-War Trials:** Documents submitted during the Nuremberg and Tokyo trials serve as a massive archive of evidence, often containing sworn testimony and seized documents that were previously classified.

### 2. Logistics Records: The Engine of War
While command documents tell historians *what* leaders wanted to do, logistics records reveal *what* they were actually able to do.
*   **Supply Chain Analysis:** By studying shipping manifests, rail timetables, and fuel consumption reports, historians can determine the limits of military operations. For instance, the German invasion of the Soviet Union (*Operation Barbarossa*) stalled not just due to Russian resistance, but because logistics records show the Wehrmacht's supply lines stretched beyond their capacity for winter fuel and fodder.
*   **Industrial Output:** Production figures from factories (e.g., the US *Lend-Lease* records or German *Rüstung* reports) allow historians to quantify the economic disparity between the Allies and the Axis. This helps explain why the war turned in favor of the Allies despite early German tactical victories.
*   **Transportation Networks:** Maps and route logs help historians reconstruct the speed of troop movements and the effectiveness of Allied bombing campaigns against key infrastructure like the French rail network or the German oil fields.

### 3. Intelligence Reports: The Fog of War
Intelligence archives (such as *Ultra* decrypts from Bletchley Park or *Magic* intercepts from the US) offer a unique perspective: what the enemy *thought* versus what they *did*.
*   **Decrypted Communications:** Historians use decrypted enemy codes to see the "true" state of mind of opposing commanders. For example, *Ultra* intercepts revealed that the Japanese were planning the attack on Midway before the battle, allowing historians to reconstruct the strategic surprise that actually occurred.
*   **Assessment vs. Reality:** Intelligence reports often contain errors or biases. Historians compare these reports with post-war accounts to understand how intelligence failures (like the surprise of Pearl Harbor) shaped the course of the war.
*   **Double Agents:** Files on agents like "Garbo" or "Dodsworth" help reconstruct the deception campaigns that misled enemy command structures, influencing the timing and location of major offensives like D-Day.

### 4. Command Documents: The Intent and Execution
Orders, after-action reports, and staff journals provide the direct link between strategy and execution.
*   **Operational Orders (OPORDs):** These documents reveal the specific objectives and timelines set by commanders. By comparing the OPORD with the actual outcome, historians can identify where plans succeeded or failed.
*   **After-Action Reports (AARs):** Written shortly after battles, these provide immediate assessments of performance. However, historians must read them critically, as they were often written to assign blame or credit. Cross-referencing AARs from opposing sides (e.g., German vs. Soviet accounts of the Battle of Stalingrad) reveals the "two-sided" reality of the battle.
*   **Staff Journals:** Daily logs of headquarters (like the German *OKW* or US *SHAEF*) track the flow of information and decision-making in real-time, showing how commanders reacted to changing circumstances.
