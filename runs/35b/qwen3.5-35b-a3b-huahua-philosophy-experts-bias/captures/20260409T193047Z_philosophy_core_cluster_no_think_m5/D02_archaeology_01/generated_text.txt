Archaeologists reconstruct the daily life of an ancient city by treating the site as a complex puzzle where architecture, artifacts, and waste deposits act as the primary pieces of evidence. This process, known as **taphonomy** (the study of what happens to an object after it is deposited) combined with **contextual analysis**, allows researchers to move beyond simple descriptions of objects to a dynamic understanding of human behavior.

Here is how each of these three pillars contributes to the reconstruction:

### 1. Architecture: The Skeleton of Daily Life
Architecture provides the physical framework within which daily life occurred. It reveals social hierarchy, economic function, and environmental adaptation.

*   **Spatial Organization and Social Hierarchy:** The layout of a city often reflects social stratification. Large, centrally located structures with high-quality materials (like stone or imported wood) usually indicate elite residences, temples, or administrative centers. In contrast, smaller, densely packed dwellings with simpler materials suggest the lives of the working class. By mapping these zones, archaeologists can visualize the "social map" of the city.
*   **Functional Zoning:** The arrangement of buildings reveals how the city functioned. Clusters of kilns indicate a pottery district; proximity to water sources suggests milling or dyeing workshops; and the location of granaries near city walls or gates points to storage and trade logistics.
*   **Construction Techniques and Materials:** The materials used (e.g., mudbrick vs. stone) and the methods of construction (e.g., post-in-ground vs. foundation walls) reveal the available technology, local climate adaptation, and the labor force required. For example, the presence of heavy timber beams in a dry region suggests extensive trade networks to bring in wood.
*   **Modifications and Repairs:** Archaeologists look for signs of renovation, such as patched walls or added rooms. These modifications tell a story of changing family sizes, economic shifts, or the need to adapt to new technologies over time.

### 2. Artifacts: The Tools and Traces of Activity
Artifacts are the tangible objects used, worn, or created by the inhabitants. They provide direct evidence of diet, technology, trade, and personal habits.

*   **Diet and Nutrition:** The most common artifacts related to food are **pottery shards** and **bone fragments**.
    *   **Residue Analysis:** Scientists can analyze microscopic residues inside pottery to determine if it held wine, oil, grain, or meat.
    *   **Faunal Remains:** The types of animal bones found (e.g., domestic cattle vs. wild deer) and their cut marks reveal what people ate and how they butchered animals.
    *   **Plant Remains:** Charred seeds and pollen found in soil samples (paleobotany) reveal the crops grown and the wild plants gathered.
*   **Technology and Craft:** Tools such as loom weights, chisels, or spindle whorls indicate specific crafts. The presence of imported materials (like obsidian, lapis lazuli, or specific glass types) reveals trade networks and the city's economic reach.
*   **Personal Adornment and Status:** Jewelry, beads, cosmetic containers (kohl pots), and clothing fasteners provide insight into personal grooming, fashion trends, and individual status. The quality and rarity of these items help distinguish between the wealthy and the poor.
*   **Wear Patterns:** Microscopic analysis of tool handles or shoe soles can reveal how often an object was used and the specific motions involved, offering clues about the intensity of labor.

### 3. Waste Deposits: The "Time Capsules" of Routine
Waste deposits (often called **middens** or **dump layers**) are perhaps the most honest record of daily life because they contain the "leftovers" that were discarded rather than curated.

*   **The "Trash" Narrative:** While elite burials or temple offerings are curated, waste represents the mundane reality. A midden might contain broken pottery, food scraps, and discarded tools. The sheer volume of waste indicates population density and consumption rates.
*   **Stratigraphy and Chronology:** Waste layers are deposited over time. By analyzing the **stratigraphy** (layers of soil), archaeologists can create a timeline. Lower layers represent older periods, while upper layers represent later ones. Changes in the types of waste found in different layers can show shifts in diet, the introduction of new goods, or changes in manufacturing techniques.
*   **Health and Disease:** Waste deposits often contain human feces (coprolites) or sewage. Analyzing these can reveal parasites, diseases, and nutritional deficiencies, providing a biological picture of the population's health.
*   **Ritual and Disposal:** Sometimes waste was not just thrown away but buried intentionally. The location of waste pits (e.g., near a house vs. in a public square) can indicate social norms regarding cleanliness and privacy.
