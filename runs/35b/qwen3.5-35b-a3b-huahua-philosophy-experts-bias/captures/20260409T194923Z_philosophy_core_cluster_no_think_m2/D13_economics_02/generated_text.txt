John Maynard Keynes (1883–1946) is widely considered the most influential economist of the 20th century. His work fundamentally changed how governments understand and manage their economies, shifting the focus from the self-correcting nature of markets to the active role of the state in stabilizing economic cycles.

Here is why Keynes mattered to economics and macroeconomic policy:

### 1. The Birth of Modern Macroeconomics
Before Keynes, classical economics (dominated by thinkers like Adam Smith and David Ricardo) assumed that markets naturally tended toward full employment. The prevailing view was that if there was unemployment, wages would eventually fall, making labor cheaper and encouraging hiring until the labor market cleared.

In his seminal 1936 work, **"The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money,"** Keynes challenged this. He argued that the economy could get stuck in a state of **persistent underemployment equilibrium**. He introduced the concept that aggregate demand (total spending in the economy) is the primary driving force of economic activity. If demand is low, businesses produce less, hire fewer people, and wages don't necessarily fall fast enough to restore full employment.

### 2. The Role of Government Intervention
Keynes provided the theoretical justification for **fiscal policy**. He argued that during a recession, when private sector spending (consumption and investment) dries up, the government should step in to fill the gap.
*   **Counter-Cyclical Policy:** Governments should spend more (or cut taxes) during downturns to stimulate demand, and save or spend less during booms to cool down inflation.
*   **The Multiplier Effect:** Keynes theorized that an initial injection of government spending would have a magnified effect on the overall economy. For example, if the government builds a bridge, the workers get paid, spend that money at local shops, and those shop owners spend it elsewhere, creating a ripple effect that generates more total income than the original investment.

### 3. Explaining the Great Depression
The Great Depression (1929–1939) was a crisis that classical economics struggled to explain. Why did unemployment remain so high for so long? Why didn't wages just drop and fix the problem?
Keynes argued that during a crisis, **animal spirits** (business confidence) collapse. Even if interest rates are low, businesses might not borrow to invest because they are pessimistic about the future. In this scenario, monetary policy (lowering interest rates) might not be enough. Therefore, direct government spending was necessary to jumpstart the economy. This framework provided a roadmap for how to escape the Great Depression.

### 4. The "Keynesian Consensus" (1945–1970s)
Following World War II, Keynesian economics became the dominant paradigm in the Western world.
*   **The Bretton Woods System:** Keynes played a key role in designing the post-war international financial order, helping to establish the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank.
*   **Full Employment:** Governments in the US, UK, and Europe adopted the goal of maintaining full employment as a primary policy objective, using budget deficits as a tool to achieve it.
*   **The Golden Age:** This era saw unprecedented economic growth and stability in the West, often attributed to the application of Keynesian demand management.

### 5. Legacy and Evolution
While the "Keynesian Consensus" faced challenges in the 1970s due to **stagflation** (high inflation combined with high unemployment), which classical monetarists like Milton Friedman argued Keynesianism couldn't solve, Keynes' core insights remain foundational:
*   **Crisis Management:** When the 2008 Global Financial Crisis hit, the world returned to Keynesian tools. Massive government bailouts, stimulus packages (like the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act), and quantitative easing were all rooted in the idea of boosting aggregate demand.
*   **Central Bank Mandates:** Modern central banks (like the Federal Reserve) still operate with a dual mandate that reflects Keynesian concerns: maximizing employment and stabilizing prices.

### Summary
John Maynard mattered because he moved economics from a study of **long-run supply** (what we can produce) to a study of **short-run demand** (what we buy). He gave policymakers the tools to smooth out the boom-and-bust cycles of capitalism, transforming the government from a passive observer into an active manager of the economy.
