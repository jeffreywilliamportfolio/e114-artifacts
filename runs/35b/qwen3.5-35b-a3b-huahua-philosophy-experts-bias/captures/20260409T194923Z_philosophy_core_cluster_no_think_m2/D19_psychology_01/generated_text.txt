Memory is a dynamic, reconstructive system rather than a static recording device. It allows us to take in information, keep it for later use, and bring it back into conscious awareness. This process is generally broken down into three core stages: **encoding**, **storage**, and **retrieval**, all of which are influenced by various **biases**.

Here is a detailed breakdown of how memory works:

### 1. Encoding: Getting Information In
Encoding is the process of transforming sensory input into a form that the brain can use. Without effective encoding, information is lost before it even enters long-term memory.

*   **Sensory Memory**: Information first hits our senses (sight, sound, touch). This lasts for a fraction of a second to a few seconds (e.g., iconic memory for visual, echoic memory for auditory).
*   **Attention**: To move from sensory memory to short-term memory, you must pay attention. If you don't focus, the information fades.
*   **Types of Encoding**:
    *   **Visual**: Converting information into mental images.
    *   **Acoustic**: Converting information into sound (e.g., repeating a phone number).
    *   **Semantic**: Converting information into meaning. This is the most effective form of encoding for long-term retention.
*   **Elaboration**: Connecting new information to existing knowledge (e.g., "This new fact reminds me of...") significantly improves encoding.

### 2. Storage: Keeping Information
Storage is the retention of encoded information over time. It is not a single location but a distributed network across the brain.

*   **Short-Term (Working) Memory**:
    *   Holds a small amount of information (about 7 ± 2 items) for a brief period (seconds to minutes).
    *   It is active; you use it to manipulate information (like doing mental math).
    *   Without rehearsal, information decays quickly.
*   **Long-Term Memory (LTM)**:
    *   Has a virtually unlimited capacity and duration.
    *   **Declarative (Explicit) Memory**: Facts and events.
        *   *Episodic*: Personal experiences (your first day of school).
        *   *Semantic*: General knowledge (Paris is the capital of France).
    *   **Non-Declarative (Implicit) Memory**: Skills and conditioned responses (riding a bike, typing).
*   **Consolidation**: This is the process where short-term memories are stabilized into long-term memories. It often happens during sleep, where the brain replays neural patterns to strengthen connections (synaptic plasticity).

### 3. Retrieval: Getting Information Out
Retrieval is the process of bringing stored information back into conscious awareness. It is often more fragile than storage.

*   **Cues**: Retrieval is usually triggered by cues. These can be external (a smell, a song) or internal (a thought, an emotion).
*   **Reconstruction**: Unlike a video file, memory is **reconstructive**. When you retrieve a memory, your brain pieces it together from fragments. This means memories can change slightly every time you recall them.
*   **Context-Dependent Memory**: You remember things better when the context of retrieval matches the context of encoding (e.g., remembering a word better if you learned it underwater and are tested underwater).
*   **State-Dependent Memory**: You remember things better when your internal state (mood, drug state) matches the state you were in when you encoded the memory.

### 4. Memory Bias: The Distortions
Because memory is reconstructive, it is prone to errors and biases. These are not necessarily "bugs" but often features that help the brain prioritize what is important.

*   **Confirmation Bias**: We tend to remember information that confirms our existing beliefs and forget information that contradicts them.
*   **Hindsight Bias**: The "I knew it all along" effect. After an event occurs, we remember our prediction as being more accurate than it actually was.
*   **The Misinformation Effect**: When post-event information (like a leading question from a witness) alters the original memory.
*   **Emotional Bias**: Highly emotional events (flashbulb memories) are often remembered vividly, but the specific details can be surprisingly inaccurate.
*   **Recency and Primacy Effects**: In a list of items, we tend to remember the first few (primacy) and the last few (recency) best, while the middle items are often forgotten.

### Summary
Memory is an active, evolving process. It starts with **encoding** (focusing and making meaning), moves to **storage** (consolidating via sleep and repetition), and ends with **retrieval** (reconstructing the memory based on cues). Throughout
