Synaptic transmission is the fundamental process by which neurons communicate with one another (or with muscles and glands) to transmit signals throughout the nervous system. It is a highly coordinated sequence of electrical and chemical events.

Here is a detailed breakdown of how it works, covering the key components and the concept of plasticity.

### 1. The Structure: The Synapse
Before transmission occurs, it is important to understand the physical gap involved.
*   **Presynaptic Neuron:** The neuron sending the signal.
*   **Synaptic Cleft:** A microscopic gap (20–40 nanometers) between the two neurons.
*   **Postsynaptic Neuron:** The neuron receiving the signal.
*   **Synaptic Vesicles:** Small sacs within the presynaptic terminal that store neurotransmitters.

### 2. The Process of Synaptic Transmission
The transmission of a signal from one neuron to another generally follows these steps:

#### Step 1: Action Potential Arrival
An electrical signal (action potential) travels down the axon of the presynaptic neuron and reaches the axon terminal.

#### Step 2: Calcium Influx
The depolarization caused by the action potential opens **voltage-gated calcium ($Ca^{2+}$) channels**. Calcium ions rush into the presynaptic terminal because their concentration is higher outside the cell.

#### Step 3: Vesicle Fusion and Exocytosis
The influx of calcium triggers synaptic vesicles to move toward the presynaptic membrane. They fuse with the membrane and release their contents (neurotransmitters) into the synaptic cleft via **exocytosis**.

#### Step 4: Diffusion and Binding
The neurotransmitters diffuse across the synaptic cleft and bind to specific **receptors** on the postsynaptic membrane. This binding is highly specific, like a key fitting into a lock.

#### Step 5: Postsynaptic Potential Generation
The binding of the neurotransmitter causes a change in the postsynaptic neuron's membrane potential:
*   **Excitatory Postsynaptic Potential (EPSP):** If the receptor opens ion channels that let positive ions (like $Na^+$) in, the neuron becomes more positive (depolarized), making it *more likely* to fire an action potential.
*   **Inhibitory Postsynaptic Potential (IPSP):** If the receptor opens channels for negative ions (like $Cl^-$) or lets positive ions ($K^+$) out, the neuron becomes more negative (hyperpolarized), making it *less likely* to fire.

#### Step 6: Signal Integration
The postsynaptic neuron sums up all the EPSPs and IPSPs arriving from thousands of other synapses. If the total depolarization at the **axon hillock** reaches a specific threshold, a new action potential is generated.

#### Step 7: Termination
To prevent continuous firing, the signal must be stopped. This happens via:
*   **Reuptake:** Transporters pump the neurotransmitter back into the presynaptic neuron (e.g., serotonin, dopamine).
*   **Enzymatic Degradation:** Enzymes in the cleft break down the neurotransmitter (e.g., acetylcholinesterase breaks down acetylcholine).
*   **Diffusion:** The neurotransmitter drifts away from the synapse.

### 3. Key Components

#### Neurotransmitters
These are the chemical messengers. They are categorized by their effect:
*   **Excitatory:** Glutamate (the primary excitatory neurotransmitter in the brain).
*   **Inhibitory:** GABA (Gamma-aminobutyric acid) and Glycine.
*   **Modulatory:** Dopamine, Serotonin, Norepinephrine, and Acetylcholine. These often regulate mood, arousal, and attention rather than directly triggering a single spike.

#### Receptors
Receptors determine how the postsynaptic neuron responds. There are two main types:
1.  **Ionotropic Receptors (Ligand-Gated Ion Channels):**
    *   These are fast-acting.
    *   The receptor *is* the ion channel. When the neurotransmitter binds, the channel opens immediately, allowing ions to flow.
    *   *Example:* NMDA and AMPA receptors for glutamate.
2.  **Metabotropic Receptors (G-Protein Coupled Receptors):**
    *   These are slower but have longer-lasting effects.
    *   Binding activates an internal G-protein, which triggers a cascade of intracellular events (second messengers) that can open/close channels or alter gene expression.
    *   *Example:* Dopamine receptors, Serotonin receptors.
