Historians reconstruct the causes and course of World War II not by relying on a single source, but by engaging in a rigorous process of **triangulation**. They cross-reference conflicting accounts from archives, logistics records, intelligence reports, and command documents to build a coherent narrative that accounts for both the "fog of war" and the strategic intent of the belligerents.

Here is how historians utilize these specific categories of evidence to reconstruct the war:

### 1. Archives: The Political and Diplomatic Context
Archives provide the "why" behind the war. They contain the raw materials of decision-making, including diplomatic cables, personal diaries, and government minutes.

*   **Reconstructing Causes:** Historians analyze pre-war diplomatic correspondence (e.g., the *Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact* negotiations or the *Munich Agreement* minutes) to understand the miscalculations and alliances that led to conflict. By reading the private diaries of leaders (like Churchill, Hitler, or Stalin), historians can distinguish between public rhetoric and private strategic intent.
*   **The "Winning" Bias:** A critical task in archival research is identifying bias. Archives are often curated by the victors. Historians must look for "silences" or gaps in the records of the defeated powers to reconstruct a balanced view of the war's origins.

### 2. Logistics Records: The Material Reality
While political archives show what leaders *wanted* to happen, logistics records show what was *actually* possible. These include shipping manifests, fuel consumption reports, rail timetables, and inventory lists.

*   **The Limits of Strategy:** Historians use logistics data to explain why certain campaigns succeeded or failed. For example, the German invasion of the Soviet Union (*Operation Barbarossa*) is often analyzed through the lens of rail gauge differences and fuel shortages. Logistics records reveal that the Wehrmacht's failure was not just tactical but structural; they simply could not move enough supplies to sustain the advance.
*   **Industrial Capacity:** By comparing production records (e.g., US tank production vs. German tank production), historians can quantify the "economic war." This helps explain the shift in momentum in 1943–1944, where Allied industrial output overwhelmed Axis capacity.

### 3. Intelligence Reports: The Fog of War
Intelligence reports (such as British *Ultra* decrypts, American *Magic* intercepts, or German *Abwehr* assessments) offer a unique window into what commanders *thought* they knew versus reality.

*   **Reconstructing Decision-Making:** Historians compare intelligence assessments with the orders issued by commanders. If a commander ignored a clear intelligence warning, historians investigate why (e.g., overconfidence, political pressure, or skepticism of the source).
*   **The "Double Game":** Intelligence records often reveal deception. For instance, the *Operation Bodyguard* documents show how the Allies fed false information to the Germans about the D-Day landing site. By analyzing the German reaction to these reports, historians can reconstruct the psychological state of the enemy command.
*   **Declassification:** Much of WWII intelligence was classified for decades. Historians often rely on recently declassified files to correct long-held myths, such as the true extent of the Battle of Midway or the specific details of the atomic bomb's development.

### 4. Command Documents: The Operational Narrative
Command documents include operational orders (*OpOrders*), after-action reports, and staff diaries. These are the "how" of the war.

*   **The Chain of Execution:** Command documents trace the flow of orders from high command down to the front lines. Historians use these to identify where communication broke down. For example, analyzing the *OKW* (German High Command) directives versus the actual orders received by field generals reveals the disconnect between Hitler's strategic vision and the reality on the ground.
*   **After-Action Reports (AARs):** These are crucial for reconstructing the "course" of battles. While often written to justify decisions, AARs contain detailed timelines of troop movements, casualty figures, and terrain analysis. Historians cross-reference AARs from opposing sides to create a 360-degree view of a battle, correcting for the tendency of each side to exaggerate their own success and minimize their losses.
