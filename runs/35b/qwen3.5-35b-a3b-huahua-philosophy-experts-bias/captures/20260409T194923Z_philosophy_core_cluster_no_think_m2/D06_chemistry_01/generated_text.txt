Chemical bonding is the force that holds atoms together to form molecules and compounds. It arises from the electrostatic attraction between positively charged nuclei and negatively charged electrons. The specific type of bond formed depends on the elements involved and how they interact to achieve a more stable electron configuration (usually a full outer shell, known as the octet rule).

Here is how chemical bonding works across the three primary types: **Ionic**, **Covalent**, and **Metallic**.

### 1. Ionic Bonding: The Transfer of Electrons
Ionic bonding typically occurs between a **metal** (which wants to lose electrons) and a **non-metal** (which wants to gain electrons).

*   **Mechanism**: One atom transfers one or more electrons to another atom.
    *   The metal becomes a positively charged ion (**cation**).
    *   The non-metal becomes a negatively charged ion (**anion**).
*   **The Force**: The bond is formed by the strong **electrostatic attraction** between the oppositely charged ions.
*   **Structure**: These bonds usually create a giant **lattice structure** (a 3D grid) rather than discrete molecules.
*   **Properties**:
    *   High melting and boiling points (due to strong lattice forces).
    *   Often soluble in water.
    *   Conduct electricity when melted or dissolved (ions are free to move), but not when solid.
*   **Example**: Table salt (**NaCl**). Sodium (Na) gives an electron to Chlorine (Cl), creating $Na^+$ and $Cl^-$ ions that stick together.

### 2. Covalent Bonding: The Sharing of Electrons
Covalent bonding typically occurs between two **non-metals** with similar electronegativities (tendency to attract electrons).

*   **Mechanism**: Instead of transferring electrons, the atoms **share** pairs of electrons to fill their outer shells.
*   **The Force**: The bond is the attraction between the shared electrons and the nuclei of both atoms.
*   **Variations**:
    *   **Non-polar Covalent**: Electrons are shared equally (e.g., $O_2$, $H_2$).
    *   **Polar Covalent**: Electrons are shared unequally, creating partial charges (e.g., $H_2O$).
*   **Structure**: These bonds form distinct **molecules**.
*   **Properties**:
    *   Generally lower melting and boiling points than ionic compounds (intermolecular forces are weaker than the bonds themselves).
    *   Often poor conductors of electricity (no free ions or electrons).
*   **Example**: Water (**$H_2O$**). Oxygen shares electrons with two Hydrogen atoms to complete their shells.

### 3. Metallic Bonding: The "Sea" of Electrons
Metallic bonding occurs between atoms of the same **metal** or in alloys.

*   **Mechanism**: Metal atoms release their outer (valence) electrons, which become delocalized. These electrons float freely throughout the structure.
*   **The Force**: The bond is the attraction between the **positive metal ions** (the nuclei with inner shells) and the **sea of delocalized negative electrons**.
*   **Structure**: A lattice of positive ions embedded in a cloud of electrons.
*   **Properties**:
    *   **Malleable and Ductile**: The layers of ions can slide over each other without breaking the bond because the electron sea adjusts.
    *   **Conductivity**: Excellent conductors of heat and electricity because the electrons move freely.
    *   **Luster**: The electrons absorb and re-emit light.
*   **Example**: Copper (**Cu**) or Iron (**Fe**).

### Summary Comparison

| Feature | Ionic Bonding | Covalent Bonding | Metallic Bonding |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Participants** | Metal + Non-metal | Non-metal + Non-metal | Metal + Metal |
| **Electron Action** | **Transferred** | **Shared** | **Delocalized** (Sea of electrons) |
| **Bond Strength** | Very Strong (Lattice) | Strong (within molecule) | Variable (usually strong) |
| **State at Room Temp** | Solid (Crystalline) | Gas, Liquid, or Solid | Solid (usually) |
| **Conductivity** | Conducts when liquid/dissolved | Poor conductor | Excellent conductor |
