Constitutional law serves as the foundational framework for a legal system, defining the structure of government, the scope of individual liberties, and the mechanisms for resolving disputes. It functions by establishing a hierarchy of authority that organizes **powers**, **rights**, and **judicial review** in a specific, interlocking manner.

Here is how constitutional law operates in these three areas:

### 1. Organizing Powers (The Structure of Government)
Constitutional law organizes powers primarily through the concept of **separation of powers** and **checks and balances**. It dictates who has the authority to act and limits the scope of that authority.

*   **Allocation of Authority:** The constitution assigns specific functions to different branches of government (Legislative, Executive, and Judicial). For example, it grants the legislature the power to make laws, the executive the power to enforce them, and the judiciary the power to interpret them.
*   **Federalism:** In systems with multiple levels of government (like the US), constitutional law organizes powers by distinguishing between **national (federal)** powers and **state/local** powers. It defines which powers are exclusive to the federal government, which are shared, and which are reserved for the states.
*   **Limitations on Power:** It acts as a constraint. A power is only valid if it is exercised within the boundaries set by the constitution. If a branch exceeds its constitutional authority (e.g., the President acting without legislative approval), the action is considered *ultra vires* (beyond the power) and may be voided.

### 2. Organizing Rights (The Sphere of Liberty)
Constitutional law organizes rights by establishing a **hierarchy of values** and defining the boundaries between public authority and private liberty.

*   **Enumeration of Rights:** Constitutions often list specific rights (e.g., freedom of speech, due process, equal protection) that are considered fundamental. These rights act as "trumps" against government interference.
*   **Negative vs. Positive Rights:**
    *   *Negative Rights:* These are restrictions on government power (e.g., "The government shall not pass a law..."). They protect the individual from the state.
    *   *Positive Rights:* These are obligations on the government to provide services or protections (e.g., the right to a fair trial or the right to vote).
*   **The "State of Nature" vs. Civil Society:** Constitutional law creates the transition from a theoretical natural state to a civil society. It organizes rights by balancing individual freedom with the need for order. It defines the "public square" where rights are exercised and the "private sphere" where rights are protected from interference.

### 3. Judicial Review (The Mechanism of Enforcement)
Judicial review is the mechanism that ensures the other two elements (powers and rights) are respected. It is the process by which courts determine whether a government action or a law is consistent with the constitution.

*   **The Supreme Court as Arbiter:** In many systems, the judiciary (often the Supreme Court) is designated as the ultimate interpreter of the constitution. This allows the courts to review the actions of the legislature and the executive.
*   **Review of Legislation:** Judicial review allows courts to strike down laws passed by the legislature if they conflict with the constitution. This is often referred to as the "supremacy of the constitution."
*   **Review of Executive Actions:** Courts review administrative actions to ensure they were taken within the scope of authority granted by the constitution or statutory law.
*   **Remedy for Rights Violations:** When rights are infringed, judicial review provides the remedy. If a law violates a constitutional right, the court declares the law invalid or grants relief to the affected individual.

### The Interlocking System
These three elements work together in a cycle:

1.  **The Constitution** sets the rules (Powers and Rights).
2.  **The Government** acts within those rules.
3.  **The Judiciary** reviews the government's actions against the rules.

If the judiciary finds that the government has overstepped its **powers** or infringed on **rights**, it uses **judicial review** to correct the balance, often by declaring the action void or ordering a specific remedy. This ensures that the constitution remains the "higher law" that governs the lower laws and the actions of the state.

### Summary
In essence, constitutional law is the **operating manual** for a political system. It tells the government **what** it can do (powers), **how** it must treat the people (rights), and **who** decides if the government has made a mistake (judicial review).
