Epistemology is the branch of philosophy that investigates the nature, sources, and limits of **knowledge** (*episteme*) and **belief** (*doxa*). It is often defined as the study of "justified true belief," though it has evolved to address the complexities of what makes a belief count as knowledge rather than mere opinion.

Here is how epistemology functions as a field concerned with justification, belief, and knowledge:

### 1. The Core Problem: The Definition of Knowledge
Epistemology begins with the question: **What is knowledge?**
For centuries, the standard definition was the **JTB (Justified True Belief)** theory, proposed by Plato. This theory suggests that for a person $P$ to know that proposition $X$ is true, three conditions must be met:
1.  **Truth:** $X$ is true.
2.  **Belief:** $P$ believes that $X$ is true.
3.  **Justification:** $P$ has adequate justification for believing that $X$ is true.

Epistemology works by analyzing these three components to see if they are sufficient or necessary for knowledge.

### 2. The Role of Justification
Justification is the bridge between a mere belief and knowledge. Epistemology explores the nature of this bridge through several major frameworks:

*   **Foundationalism:** Beliefs are like a building. Some beliefs are "basic" (self-evident or sensory) and serve as the foundation for all other beliefs. Justification comes from these basic foundations.
*   **Coherentism:** A belief is justified if it fits coherently within a larger system of beliefs. Truth is a matter of consistency and logical integration.
*   **Reliabilism:** A belief is justified if it is produced by a reliable cognitive process (e.g., vision, memory, or logic). If the process usually yields true results, the belief is justified.
*   **Internalism vs. Externalism:**
    *   *Internalism:* Justification depends on the internal mental states of the person (what they see, hear, or reason).
    *   *Externalism:* Justification depends on external factors (like the reliability of the process or the causal link to the world).

### 3. The Role of Belief
Epistemology treats belief as a mental state that has "fidelity" or "direction of fit."
*   **Propositional Attitude:** To believe is to take a stance on how the world is. If the world matches the belief, the belief is true; if it doesn't, the belief is false.
*   **Degrees of Belief:** Epistemology also distinguishes between *full belief* (certainty) and *credence* (probability).
*   **The "Get" Problem:** A major challenge in epistemology is the **Gettier Problem** (named after philosopher Edmund Gettier). Gettier argued that JTB is not sufficient for knowledge. He provided counter-examples where a person has a justified true belief, but the justification seems to rely on a lucky coincidence or a false lemma, preventing it from being "knowledge."
    *   *Example:* A person believes "there is a plow plow" because they saw a plow plow. It turns out there is a plow plow, but the person's justification was based on a faulty reading of a map. The belief is true and justified, but it seems to lack the necessary connection to reality to be *knowledge*.

### 4. The Goal: Distinguishing Knowledge from Opinion
The ultimate function of epistemology is to separate **Knowledge** (which is robust and secure) from **Opinion** (which is merely probable or subjective).
*   **Skepticism:** Epistemology often confronts skepticism—the idea that we might not have knowledge of the external world (e.g., "How do we know we aren't just brains in a vat?").
*   **Skepticism's Challenge:** If knowledge requires absolute justification, and our senses can be deceiving, can we ever truly claim to know anything?

### Summary
Epistemology works by:
1.  **Defining** the criteria for knowledge (Truth, Belief, Justification).
2.  **Analyzing** the structure of justification (how beliefs connect to reality).
3.  **Testing** these definitions against counter-examples (Gettier cases).
4.  **Resolving** the tension between skepticism and our everyday claims to knowledge.
