Historians reconstruct the causes and course of World War II through a rigorous process of **source criticism**, **triangulation**, and **contextual analysis**. Because the war involved multiple belligerents with competing narratives, the "truth" is rarely found in a single document but rather in the gaps and overlaps between them.

Here is how historians utilize specific categories of primary sources to build a comprehensive historical narrative:

### 1. Archives: The Political and Diplomatic Context
Archives provide the "why" behind the war. They contain diplomatic cables, treaties, and government correspondence that reveal the strategic thinking of political leaders.

*   **Diplomatic Correspondence:** Historians analyze letters between heads of state (e.g., the correspondence between Hitler, Stalin, and Roosevelt) to trace the breakdown of alliances and the escalation of tensions. For example, the *Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact* archives reveal the secret protocols that divided Eastern Europe, explaining the initial ease of the German invasion of Poland.
*   **Post-War Trials and Testimonies:** Documents from the Nuremberg and Tokyo trials, along with captured German diaries (like those of Hermann Göring or Joachim von Ribbentrop), offer insight into the internal decision-making processes that led to the war.
*   **Cross-Referencing:** Historians compare the public statements of leaders with their private memos to identify discrepancies between what was said to the public and what was decided in the war room.

### 2. Logistics Records: The Material Reality
While archives explain the *intent*, logistics records explain the *capacity* and the *limitations* of the war effort. These records often determine the outcome of battles before they are even fought.

*   **Supply Chain Analysis:** By examining shipping manifests, fuel consumption reports, and rail schedules, historians can determine why certain offensives stalled. For instance, the German failure in the Soviet Union is often attributed to logistical overextension; records show that the Wehrmacht's supply lines stretched beyond their capacity to maintain fuel and ammunition.
*   **Industrial Output:** Production statistics from factories (e.g., the number of tanks produced in the US vs. Germany) allow historians to quantify the "economic war." This helps explain the shift in momentum after 1942, when Allied industrial superiority became overwhelming.
*   **Manpower and Morale:** Personnel rosters and casualty reports help historians reconstruct the human cost and the sustainability of armies over time.

### 3. Intelligence Reports: The Fog of War
Intelligence reports (such as the British *Ultra* decrypts or American *Magic* intercepts) provide a unique window into what commanders *thought* they knew versus what was actually happening.

*   **Decryption and Decoding:** Historians study decrypted messages (like the Enigma ciphers) to see how the Allies anticipated German movements. This reveals the "intelligence gap"—where the Allies knew the enemy's plans and where they were blindsided.
*   **The "Lie" Factor:** Intelligence is often subjective. Historians must distinguish between raw data (a radio intercept) and the interpretation of that data by analysts. A report might claim an enemy division is at location X, but if the logistics records show the division lacks fuel, the historian knows the report is likely exaggerated or mistaken.
*   **Deception Operations:** Records of operations like *Operation Bodyguard* (the D-Day deception) show how intelligence was used not just to gather data, but to manipulate the enemy's perception.

### 4. Command Documents: The Operational Narrative
Command documents (orders of battle, after-action reports, and field orders) reconstruct the "how" of the war—the tactical and operational execution.

*   **Orders of Battle (ORBAT):** These documents list the specific units, commanders, and equipment assigned to a theater. They allow historians to map the exact composition of forces at the time of a battle.
*   **After-Action Reports (AARs):** Written by commanders shortly after a battle, these provide immediate assessments of success and failure. However, historians treat them with caution, as they are often written to justify decisions or shift blame.
*   **Diaries and Memoirs:** Personal accounts from field commanders (e.g., Erwin Rommel or George Patton) provide the "ground truth" of the soldier's experience, contrasting with the "bird's eye view" of high command.

### The Method of Triangulation
The most critical skill in this reconstruction is **triangulation**. Historians rarely trust a single source. Instead, they cross-reference:

1.  **The German Perspective:** Did the German command *plan* to attack Stalingrad? (Command documents)
2.  **The Soviet Perspective:** Did the Soviets *know* about the attack? (Intelligence reports)
3.  **The Material Reality:** Did the Germans have the *fuel* to sustain the attack
