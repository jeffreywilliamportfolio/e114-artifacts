Constitutional law is the foundational framework that organizes the **powers**, **rights**, and **judicial review** of a society. It serves as the "constitution" of the state, defining the structure of government and the boundaries of individual liberty.

Here is how constitutional law functions in organizing these three critical elements:

### 1. Organizing Powers (The Structure of Government)
Constitutional law defines the **authority** of the government by establishing the hierarchy of control. It organizes powers by:
*   **Defining Branches:** It separates the government into distinct branches (e.g., Executive, Legislative, Judicial).
*   **Establishing Limits:** It sets the boundaries of what each branch can do. For example, the Constitution often limits the legislative power to the "making of laws" while restricting the executive power to the "execution of laws."
*   **Allocation of Authority:** It determines who holds the highest power (e.g., the President vs. the Parliament) and how that power is distributed across different levels of administration.

### 1. Organizing Rights (The Boundaries of Liberty)
Constitutional law organizes **rights** by establishing the "rights of the citizenry" and the "rights of the government." It functions by:
*   **Defining Rights:** It creates a list of fundamental rights (e.g., freedom of speech, right to vote, right to property).
*   **Establishing Limits:** It sets the boundaries of what each branch can do. For example, the Constitution often limits the legislative power to the "making of laws" while restricting the executive power to the "execution of laws."
*   **Allocation of Authority:** It determines who holds the highest power (e.g., the President vs. the Parliament) and how that power is distributed across different levels of administration.
