Public key cryptography is a foundational system that enables secure communication, authentication, and trust in digital networks. It operates through a set of mechanisms that include **key exchange**, **signatures**, and **trust**. Let’s explore each of these components.

---

### **1. Key Exchange**

**Key exchange** refers to the process of distributing and agreeing upon cryptographic keys between two parties. In public key cryptography, one party typically holds a **public key** (used for encryption or verification), while the other party holds a **private key** (used for decryption or signing).

- **Public Key**: A key that can be shared with others, used to encrypt messages or verify signatures.
- **Private Key**: A key that is kept by the sender, used to decrypt messages or create signatures.

**Key exchange** is often done through:
- **Digital certificates** (e.g., RSA, PEM format)
- **Key agreement protocols** (e.g., Diffie–Hellman)
- **Trusting authority** (e.g., trusted third parties like CA, or trusted peers)

This allows parties to communicate securely without needing to physically exchange keys.

---

### **2. Signatures**

**Signatures** are cryptographic proofs that verify the authenticity of a message or document. They are created using the **private key** and verified using the **public key**.

- **Signing**: A sender uses their private key to generate a signature on a message.
- **Verification**: A receiver uses the sender’s public key to verify the signature.

**Signatures** are used to:
- **Authenticate identity** (e.g., digital signatures in legal documents)
- **Verify integrity** (e.g., ensuring a message hasn been tampered)
- **Establish trust** (e.g., verifying a sender's identity)

---

### **3. Trust**

**Trust** is the foundational belief that a sender is reliable, and that their public key is correctly associated with their identity.

- **Trust is established through**:
- **Digital certificates** (e.g., trusted CA, or trusted peers)
- **Key agreement protocols** (e.g., Diffie–Hellman)
- **Trusting authority** (e.g., trusted third parties like CA, or trusted peers)

**Trust** is often done through:
- **Digital certificates** (e.g., RSA, PEM format)
- **Key agreement protocols** (e.g., Diffie–Hellman)
- **Trusting authority** (e.g., trusted third parties like CA, or trusted peers)
