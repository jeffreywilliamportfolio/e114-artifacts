Memory is a dynamic, reconstructive system rather than a static recording device. It allows us to acquire, retain, and recall information, playing a crucial role in learning, identity, and decision-making.

Here is a breakdown of how memory works through its three core stages—**encoding**, **storage**, and **retrieval**—and how **bias** influences the process.

### 1. Encoding: Getting Information In
Encoding is the process of transforming sensory input into a form that the brain can use. Without effective encoding, information is lost before it ever becomes a memory.

*   **Sensory Memory:** Information first enters the sensory register (iconic for visual, echoic for auditory). It lasts only milliseconds to seconds.
*   **Attention:** To move from sensory memory to short-term memory, you must pay attention to the stimulus.
*   **Types of Encoding:**
    *   **Visual:** Encoding images.
    *   **Acoustic:** Encoding sounds (e.g., repeating a phone number).
    *   **Semantic:** Encoding meaning. This is the most effective form for long-term retention. Connecting new information to what you already know (elaborative rehearsal) creates stronger neural pathways.

### 2. Storage: Holding Information Over Time
Storage is the retention of encoded information over time. It involves the consolidation of memories, where short-term memories are stabilized into long-term memories.

*   **Short-Term (Working) Memory:** Holds a small amount of information (about 7±2 items) for roughly 20–30 seconds. It is the "workspace" of the mind.
*   **Long-Term Memory (LTM):** Has a virtually unlimited capacity and duration.
    *   **Declarative (Explicit):** Facts and events (e.g., your first day of school).
    *   **Non-Declarative (Implicit):** Skills and conditioned responses (e.g., riding a bike).
*   **Consolidation:** This is the biological process where memories are stabilized. It often occurs during sleep, where the brain replays neural patterns to strengthen synaptic connections. The **hippocampus** is critical for this initial consolidation before memories are distributed to the **cortex** for long-term storage.

### 3. Retrieval: Getting Information Out
Retrieval is the process of bringing stored information back into conscious awareness. It is not a simple "playback" but often a reconstruction.

*   **Recall:** Retrieving information without cues (e.g., an essay exam). This is the hardest form of retrieval.
*   **Recognition:** Identifying information when presented with it (e.g., a multiple-choice test). This is generally easier because the cues are provided.
*   **Relearning:** Measuring how much faster you learn material the second time. This is often the most sensitive measure of memory retention.
*   **Cues:** Retrieval is heavily dependent on context. The **Encoding Specificity Principle** states that memory is best when the context at retrieval matches the context at encoding (e.g., remembering a word better if you hear it in the same room where you learned it).

### 4. Memory Bias: The Reconstructive Nature
Because memory is reconstructive, it is prone to errors and distortions. We do not store "files"; we store fragments that the brain pieces together every time we recall them.

*   **Confirmation Bias:** We tend to remember information that confirms our existing beliefs and forget information that contradicts them.
*   **The Misinformation Effect:** Post-event information can alter a memory. If asked leading questions after an event (e.g., "How fast were the cars going when they *smashed* into each other?"), witnesses may recall details that never happened.
*   **Hindsight Bias:** The "I knew it all along" effect, where we view past events as more predictable than they actually were, altering our memory of the original uncertainty.
*   **Emotional Bias:** Highly emotional events (flashbulb memories) are often remembered vividly, but the details surrounding them can be surprisingly inaccurate.
*   **Source Amnesia:** We remember the content of a memory but forget where it came from, leading us to attribute a memory to the wrong source (e.g., thinking you saw a news clip when you actually read about it).

### Summary
Memory is a three-step cycle: **Encoding** (input), **Storage** (maintenance), and **Retrieval** (output). It is not a perfect video recorder but a **reconstructive process** that is highly susceptible to **bias**, context, and emotional state.
