Phonology and syntax are two distinct but interconnected levels of linguistic structure. While both deal with the organization of language, they operate on different units, govern different types of rules, and serve different functions in the communication system.

Here is a comparison of the kinds of linguistic structure each studies:

### 1. The Units of Analysis
The most fundamental difference lies in the building blocks each field analyzes.

*   **Phonology** studies the structure of **sounds** (phonemes) and their patterns. It looks at how individual speech sounds (like /p/, /t/, /k/) are organized into syllables, words, and larger prosodic units. It is concerned with the abstract mental representation of sounds rather than their physical acoustic properties (which is the domain of *phonetics*).
*   **Syntax** studies the structure of **words** and how they combine to form **phrases** and **sentences**. It analyzes the hierarchical arrangement of lexical items (nouns, verbs, adjectives, etc.) and the rules that dictate their order and relationship to one another.

### 2. The Nature of the Structure
The way these units are organized differs significantly in terms of hierarchy and linearity.

*   **Phonological Structure:**
    *   **Hierarchical:** Sounds are grouped into syllables, which are grouped into feet, which are grouped into prosodic words, and so on.
    *   **Linear and Sequential:** Phonology is heavily concerned with the order of sounds (e.g., in English, the sequence /ŋ/ usually appears at the end of a syllable, not the beginning).
    *   **Feature-based:** It analyzes sounds based on distinctive features (e.g., voiced vs. unvoiced, nasal vs. oral) and how these features interact (e.g., assimilation rules where a sound changes to match a neighbor).
*   **Syntactic Structure:**
    *   **Deeply Hierarchical:** Syntax is defined by tree structures. Words group into phrases (Noun Phrases, Verb Phrases), which group into larger clauses. This hierarchy determines grammatical relationships (like subject-verb agreement) regardless of linear distance.
    *   **Dependency and Relations:** It focuses on how words depend on one another (e.g., a verb governs its object) and how constituents move or are deleted in transformational processes.
    *   **Abstract Categories:** It deals with abstract grammatical categories (Part of Speech) and their functional roles (Subject, Object, Modifier) rather than the physical properties of the words.

### 3. The Scope of Rules
The rules governing each domain address different types of constraints.

*   **Phonological Rules:** These are often **context-sensitive**. A sound might change its pronunciation depending on its neighbors.
    *   *Example:* In English, the plural suffix is pronounced /s/ after voiceless sounds (cats) but /z/ after voiced sounds (dogs). This is a phonological rule based on the environment.
*   **Syntactic Rules:** These are often **structural** and **categorical**. They dictate what can follow what, regardless of the specific meaning or sound of the words.
    *   *Example:* In English, an adjective usually precedes a noun ("the big house"), but in French, it often follows ("la maison grande"). This is a syntactic rule regarding word order.

### 4. Interface and Interaction
While distinct, phonology and syntax interact at the boundaries of linguistic processing.

*   **Prosody:** Syntax often dictates the rhythm and intonation (phonology) of a sentence. For example, a syntactic boundary (like the end of a clause) often triggers a pause or a specific pitch change in the phonological output.
*   **Phonological Constraints on Syntax:** Sometimes, the sound structure of a word can influence its syntactic behavior. For instance, in some languages, the number of syllables in a word might determine which syntactic constructions it can participate in.

### Summary Table

| Feature | Phonology | Syntax |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Unit** | Phonemes (sounds) | Words and Phrases |
| **Core Question** | How are sounds organized into meaningful patterns? | How are words organized into meaningful sentences? |
| **Structure Type** | Syllables, feet, prosodic words | Noun phrases, verb phrases, clauses |
| **Key Mechanism** | Distinctive features, assimilation, stress | Word order, agreement, movement, hierarchy |
| **Output** | Pronunciation patterns | Grammatical sentences |
