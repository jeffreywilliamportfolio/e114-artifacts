Comparing monotheistic traditions (such as Judaism, Christianity, and Islam) with non-theistic traditions (such as Buddhism, Jainism, and certain schools of Hinduism like Advaita Vedanta) reveals profound differences in how reality is perceived, how the divine is approached, and how spiritual practice is structured.

Here is a comparative analysis of these traditions across theology, the nature of the divine, and religious practice.

### 1. Theological Foundations

#### The Nature of the Divine
*   **Monotheistic Traditions:** The central axiom is the existence of a single, transcendent, and personal God (Yahweh, Allah, or the Trinity). This God is the creator, sustainer, and judge of the universe. The relationship between the human and the divine is often defined by covenant, grace, or submission. God is distinct from creation but intimately involved in it.
*   **Non-Theistic Traditions:** These traditions often lack a creator deity or a supreme ruler.
    *   **Buddhism:** Generally focuses on the *Dharma* (truth/teachings) rather than a god. While some schools (like Pure Land Buddhism) incorporate deities, the core theology is non-theistic, focusing on the nature of suffering and the path to liberation (Nirvana).
    *   **Jainism:** Emphasizes the soul (*jiva*) and the universe's mechanics of karma. Tirthankaras (spiritual teachers) are revered but are not "gods" in the sense of creators; they are exemplars who have achieved liberation.
    *   **Advaita Vedanta (Hinduism):** While Hinduism is often theistic, this school posits that the ultimate reality (*Brahman*) is an impersonal, formless consciousness, and the individual soul (*Atman*) is identical to it, rendering the distinction between "worshipper" and "God" illusory.

#### The Problem of Existence
*   **Monotheistic:** The primary problem is often **sin** or **separation** from God. Existence is a gift, and the human condition is defined by the tension between divine will and human free will. The goal is reconciliation, salvation, or submission to the divine will.
*   **Non-Theistic:** The primary problem is **suffering** (*Dukkha*) or **ignorance** (*Avidya*). Existence is characterized by impermanence and the cycle of rebirth (*Samsara*). The goal is not to please a deity but to extinguish the causes of suffering (desire, attachment, ignorance) and achieve liberation.

### 2. Religious Practice and Ritual

#### Worship vs. Meditation
*   **Monotheistic:** Practice is heavily centered on **worship** (*Latria* in Christianity, *Salah* in Islam, *Avodah* in Judaism). This involves prayer, sacrifice, liturgy, and praise directed *toward* God. The relationship is vertical: the human looks up to the divine.
*   **Non-Theistic:** Practice is often centered on **meditation**, **mindfulness**, and **ethical living**. While rituals exist (e.g., offering flowers to a Buddha statue), they are often symbolic aids to focus the mind rather than acts of petition to a deity. The relationship is often horizontal (between self and others) or internal (between the mind and its nature).

#### The Role of Scripture
*   **Monotheistic:** Scripture is viewed as the **revealed word** of God. It is authoritative, often literal, and serves as the primary source of law and doctrine (Torah, Bible, Quran). Interpretation is often guided by the belief that God spoke through prophets.
*   **Non-Theistic:** Scripture is viewed as **instructional** or **descriptive**. The texts (Tripitaka, Agamas, Upanishads) record the teachings of enlightened beings or the nature of reality. They are guides for practice rather than divine commands. The focus is on understanding the nature of the mind and the universe.

#### Community and Hierarchy
*   **Monotheistic:** Often features a strong hierarchical structure (priests, imams, rabbis) who act as intermediaries between the laity and God. The community is bound by a shared covenant or faith in a specific deity.
*   **Non-Theistic:** Hierarchy is often based on **realization** or **seniority** rather than divine appointment. In Buddhism, the Sangha (community) is a collective of practitioners. In Jainism, ascetics are revered for their discipline. The focus is on the shared pursuit of liberation rather than adherence to a specific dogma.

### 3. Key Similarities and Overlaps

Despite these differences, significant overlaps exist:
*   **Ethics:** Both traditions emphasize compassion, charity, and moral conduct.
